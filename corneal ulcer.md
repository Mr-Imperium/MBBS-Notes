# Corneal Ulcer - Complete Clinical Notes

## Definition
A corneal ulcer is a break in the corneal epithelium with underlying stromal infiltration and suppuration, representing an ophthalmological emergency that can lead to permanent vision loss if untreated.

## Anatomy Review
The cornea consists of five layers: epithelium, Bowman's layer, stroma (90% of thickness), Descemet's membrane, and endothelium. Ulcers typically involve epithelial loss with stromal infiltration.

## Etiology and Classification

### 1. Infectious Causes

**Bacterial (most common)**
- Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa (contact lens wearers)
- Streptococcus pneumoniae, Moraxella species
- Risk factors: trauma, contact lens use, dry eyes, immunosuppression

**Fungal**
- Filamentous fungi: Fusarium, Aspergillus (vegetative trauma)
- Candida species (steroid use, immunocompromised)
- More common in agricultural workers, tropical climates

**Viral**
- Herpes simplex virus (HSV) - dendritic/geographic ulcers
- Herpes zoster ophthalmicus
- Adenovirus (epidemic keratoconjunctivitis)

**Protozoal**
- Acanthamoeba (contact lens wearers, contaminated water)
- Characteristically severe pain out of proportion to clinical findings

### 2. Non-Infectious Causes
- Exposure keratopathy (lagophthalmos, Bell's palsy)
- Neurotrophic keratopathy (cranial nerve V damage)
- Dry eye syndrome (severe)
- Chemical/thermal burns
- Autoimmune disorders (rheumatoid arthritis, Sjögren's syndrome)
- Vitamin A deficiency (xerophthalmia)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Severe eye pain (pain may be minimal in neurotrophic ulcers)
- Photophobia
- Excessive lacrimation
- Decreased vision or blurred vision
- Foreign body sensation
- Purulent or mucopurulent discharge
- Blepharospasm

### Signs
- Circumcorneal or ciliary congestion
- Loss of corneal luster and transparency
- Epithelial defect with stromal infiltration
- Surrounding corneal edema
- Anterior chamber reaction (cells and flare)
- Hypopyon (sterile or septic) in severe cases
- Decreased corneal sensation (herpetic, neurotrophic)
- Conjunctival congestion and chemosis

## Specific Types

### 1. Bacterial Corneal Ulcer
**Characteristics:**
- Acute onset with rapid progression
- Well-defined stromal infiltrate with overlying epithelial defect
- Surrounding stromal edema
- Anterior chamber reaction common
- Purulent discharge

**Pseudomonas ulcer:** Greenish-yellow discharge, rapidly progressive, can perforate within 48 hours

**Pneumococcal ulcer:** Serpiginous (spreading) pattern with sharp advancing edge and healing trailing edge

### 2. Fungal Corneal Ulcer
**Characteristics:**
- Insidious onset, slow progression
- History of trauma with vegetative matter
- Dry, raised slough with feathery edges
- Satellite lesions around main ulcer
- Hypopyon with fluffy appearance
- Poor response to antibacterial therapy
- Endothelial plaque may be present

### 3. Viral (Herpetic) Corneal Ulcer
**HSV Keratitis:**
- Dendritic ulcer (early): branching pattern with terminal bulbs, fluorescein positive, rose bengal staining at edges
- Geographic ulcer (late): larger, map-like configuration
- Decreased corneal sensation (pathognomonic)
- Mild anterior chamber reaction
- May have history of recurrent episodes

**Herpes Zoster:**
- Follows trigeminal nerve distribution
- Pseudodendritic pattern (raised epithelial plaques)
- Associated skin vesicles

### 4. Acanthamoeba Keratitis
**Characteristics:**
- Contact lens wearers (poor hygiene)
- Disproportionate pain (early sign)
- Ring infiltrate (characteristic but late finding)
- Radial keratoneuritis
- Slow, progressive course
- Difficult to treat

### 5. Mooren's Ulcer (Non-infectious)
**Characteristics:**
- Chronic, painful, peripheral ulcerative keratitis
- Autoimmune etiology
- Starts peripherally, progresses circumferentially
- Undermined central edge
- No scleral involvement
- Bilateral in younger patients, unilateral in elderly

## Investigations

### Essential Tests
1. **Slit-lamp examination**
   - Assess ulcer size, depth, location, and extent
   - Check for anterior chamber reaction, hypopyon
   - Measure corneal sensation

2. **Fluorescein staining**
   - Demonstrates epithelial defect (stains green)
   - Assess for Seidel's test (aqueous leak)

3. **Corneal scraping for microbiology**
   - Before starting treatment in severe cases
   - For culture and sensitivity (bacteria, fungi)
   - KOH mount for fungi (shows hyphae/pseudohyphae)
   - Gram stain for bacteria

4. **Confocal microscopy** (if available)
   - Especially useful for Acanthamoeba (shows cysts)

5. **Blood investigations**
   - Blood sugar (diabetes mellitus)
   - HIV testing in recurrent/atypical cases

## Complications

### Early Complications
- Anterior uveitis
- Secondary glaucoma
- Descemetocele formation (Descemet's membrane prolapse)
- Corneal perforation with iris prolapse
- Endophthalmitis

### Late Complications
- Corneal scarring and opacity (leucoma)
- Irregular astigmatism
- Amblyopia (in children)
- Anterior staphyloma
- Adherent leucoma
- Phthisis bulbi (end-stage)

## Management

### General Principles
1. Identify and treat the underlying cause
2. Prevent progression and complications
3. Promote healing
4. Preserve vision

### Medical Management

**Bacterial Corneal Ulcer:**
- **Empirical therapy** (before culture results):
  - Fortified antibiotics (topical)
  - Cefazolin 5% (50 mg/ml) - Gram-positive coverage
  - Tobramycin or gentamicin 1.5% (15 mg/ml) - Gram-negative coverage
  - Frequency: Every hour initially, then taper based on response
  - Fluoroquinolones: Moxifloxacin 0.5% or gatifloxacin 0.3% (monotherapy for mild-moderate cases)

- **Specific therapy** (after culture):
  - Modify antibiotics based on sensitivity
  - Continue for at least 48 hours after complete healing

- **Adjuvant therapy:**
  - Cycloplegics: Atropine 1% or homatropine 2% (relieve ciliary spasm, prevent posterior synechiae)
  - Oral analgesics for pain
  - Avoid topical steroids in active infection

**Fungal Corneal Ulcer:**
- Natamycin 5% (drug of choice for filamentous fungi) - every hour initially
- Amphotericin B 0.15-0.3% (for Candida and deep infections)
- Voriconazole 1% (broad spectrum, good penetration)
- Oral antifungals: Fluconazole, itraconazole, or voriconazole for deep infections
- Treatment duration: 6-12 weeks minimum
- May require therapeutic penetrating keratoplasty in non-responsive cases

**Viral (Herpetic) Keratitis:**
- Topical antivirals:
  - Acyclovir 3% ointment 5 times daily
  - Ganciclovir 0.15% gel
- Oral antivirals: Acyclovir 400 mg 5 times daily or valacyclovir 500 mg TDS
- Avoid topical steroids during epithelial disease
- Steroids may be used cautiously in stromal keratitis after epithelial healing (with antiviral cover)

**Acanthamoeba Keratitis:**
- Combination therapy (difficult to treat):
  - Biguanides: Polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB) 0.02% or chlorhexidine 0.02%
  - Diamidines: Propamidine isethionate 0.1% (Brolene)
  - Both agents hourly for first 48-72 hours, then every 2 hours
- Duration: Several months (3-6 months or longer)
- May require therapeutic keratoplasty

**Mooren's Ulcer:**
- Topical steroids: Prednisolone acetate 1%
- Systemic immunosuppression: Cyclophosphamide, cyclosporine
- Conjunctival resection at limbus
- May require tissue adhesive or lamellar keratoplasty

### Surgical Management

**Indications:**
1. Impending or actual perforation
2. Non-healing ulcer despite optimal medical therapy
3. Descemetocele formation
4. Therapeutic failure

**Procedures:**
1. **Tissue adhesives (cyanoacrylate glue)**
   - For small perforations (<2 mm)
   - Temporary measure, bandage contact lens applied over glue

2. **Amniotic membrane transplantation**
   - Promotes epithelialization
   - Anti-inflammatory properties

3. **Conjunctival flap (Gunderson flap)**
   - For non-healing ulcers
   - Provides trophic support and vascularization
   - Vision sacrificed temporarily

4. **Therapeutic penetrating keratoplasty (TPK)**
   - Removes infected tissue
   - Restores corneal integrity
   - Done for large perforations, non-responsive infections
   - High risk of graft failure

5. **Lamellar keratoplasty**
   - For superficial ulcers not involving full thickness

6. **Tarsorrhaphy**
   - For exposure keratopathy
   - Temporary or permanent

### Supportive Measures
- Bed rest in severe cases
- Dark glasses for photophobia
- Avoid eye pad (may promote bacterial growth)
- Patient education about compliance
- Monitor intraocular pressure (steroid-induced glaucoma risk)

## Prognosis

**Good prognosis:**
- Small, peripheral ulcers
- Early presentation and treatment
- Bacterial etiology (with appropriate antibiotics)
- No hypopyon

**Poor prognosis:**
- Central large ulcers
- Delayed presentation
- Fungal or Acanthamoeba etiology
- Presence of hypopyon
- Immunocompromised patients
- Perforation or descemetocele

## Prevention
1. Proper contact lens hygiene (avoid tap water for cleaning)
2. Use of protective eyewear during agricultural work
3. Prompt treatment of corneal abrasions
4. Avoid self-medication with steroids
5. Regular follow-up in high-risk patients (dry eye, exposure)
6. Vitamin A supplementation in at-risk populations

## Key Points for Exams
- Corneal ulcer is an ophthalmological emergency requiring urgent treatment
- Bacterial ulcers are most common, fungal ulcers follow vegetative trauma
- Hypopyon indicates severe infection (may be sterile or septic)
- Pseudomonas can perforate cornea within 48 hours (most aggressive)
- HSV keratitis shows dendritic ulcer with decreased corneal sensation
- Acanthamoeba keratitis: disproportionate pain + ring infiltrate + contact lens history
- Fungal ulcers: feathery edges + satellite lesions + slow progression
- Fluorescein staining is essential for diagnosis
- Corneal scraping for culture before starting treatment (in severe cases)
- Fortified antibiotics are used for bacterial ulcers (not commercially available concentrations)
- Never use topical steroids in active epithelial infection
- Central corneal scarring leads to significant visual impairment
- Therapeutic keratoplasty is last resort for non-healing/perforated ulcers

## Clinical Examination Format

**On Examination:**
- Visual acuity: Reduced
- Circumcorneal congestion present
- Corneal ulcer present with stromal infiltration
- Size: ___ mm × ___ mm
- Location: Central/paracentral/peripheral
- Depth: Superficial/deep (involving ___ % of stroma)
- Edges: Well-defined/feathery/undermined
- Floor: Clean/slough present
- Surrounding corneal edema present
- Anterior chamber: Cells and flare present/Hypopyon present (height ___ mm)
- Pupil: Dilated (if cycloplegic used)
- Fundal glow: Present/absent

**Diagnosis:** Corneal ulcer (specify type if identifiable: bacterial/fungal/viral)

**Investigations planned:** Slit-lamp examination, fluorescein staining, corneal scraping for culture and sensitivity

**Management:** Medical/surgical as per protocol
