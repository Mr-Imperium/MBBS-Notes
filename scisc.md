## 1. LID DISORDERS

### Hordeolum (Stye)
- **Definition**: Acute suppurative inflammation of lid glands
- **Types**: 
  - External: Zeiss/Moll glands
  - Internal: Meibomian glands
- **Clinical**: Painful, red, tender swelling at lid margin with pointing
- **Treatment**: Hot compresses, topical antibiotics, incision & curettage if needed
- **SICS**: Differentiate from chalazion - hordeolum is acute & painful

### Chalazion
- **Definition**: Chronic granulomatous inflammation of Meibomian gland
- **Clinical**: Painless, slow-growing, hard nodule in tarsal plate
- **Treatment**: 
  - Conservative: hot compresses
  - Surgical: incision & curettage from conjunctival side
- **SICS**: Multiple/recurrent chalazion → rule out sebaceous cell carcinoma (biopsy needed)

### Blepharitis
- **Types**: 
  - Anterior: staphylococcal, seborrheic
  - Posterior: meibomian dysfunction
- **Clinical**: Lid margin inflammation, crusting, burning, itching, recurrent styes
- **Treatment**: Lid hygiene, warm compresses, antibiotic ointment, artificial tears
- **Complications**: Conjunctivitis, keratitis, chalazion, madarosis

### Ptosis
- **Types**: 
  - Congenital: simple, Marcus Gunn jaw winking
  - Acquired: myogenic, neurogenic, aponeurotic, mechanical
- **Examination**: MRD, levator function, Bell's phenomenon, Marcus Gunn test
- **Treatment**: 
  - Levator resection (good levator >4mm)
  - Frontalis sling (poor levator <4mm)
- **SICS**: Always check for amblyopia in congenital ptosis. Rule out 3rd nerve palsy, Horner, myasthenia

### Entropion
- **Types**: Congenital, involutional, spastic, cicatricial
- **Clinical**: Inturned lid margin, trichiasis causing corneal irritation
- **Treatment**: 
  - Temporary: taping
  - Permanent: everting sutures, lid repositioning
- **Complications**: Corneal abrasion, ulceration, scarring

### Ectropion
- **Types**: Congenital, involutional, paralytic, cicatricial, mechanical
- **Clinical**: Everted lid margin, epiphora, conjunctival exposure
- **Treatment**: Lubricants for mild. Surgery: lateral tarsal strip, wedge resection
- **Complications**: Exposure keratopathy, chronic conjunctivitis

## 2. CONJUNCTIVAL DISORDERS

### Bacterial Conjunctivitis
- **Etiology**: S. aureus, S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae (children)
- **Clinical**: Mucopurulent discharge, conjunctival congestion, papillae, morning lid sticking
- **Treatment**: Topical antibiotics - fluoroquinolones or chloramphenicol 2-4 hourly
- **SICS**: Hyperacute (N. gonorrhoeae) - copious purulent discharge, needs systemic antibiotics

### Viral Conjunctivitis
- **Types**: 
  - EKC (Adenovirus 8,19)
  - PCF (Adenovirus 3,7)
- **Clinical**: Watery discharge, follicles, preauricular lymphadenopathy, pseudomembrane
- **EKC**: Subepithelial infiltrates develop after 7-10 days, may last months
- **Treatment**: Supportive - cold compresses, lubricants. Steroids only for SEI affecting vision
- **SICS**: Highly contagious! Isolate patient. Avoid steroid misuse

### Allergic Conjunctivitis
- **Types**: SAC, PAC, VKC, AKC, GPC
- **VKC**: Boys, bilateral, spring/summer, shield ulcer, Tranta's dots, cobblestone papillae
- **Treatment**: 
  - Cold compresses, mast cell stabilizers, antihistamines
  - VKC: steroids (short course), cyclosporine
- **SICS**: Shield ulcer in VKC needs aggressive treatment to prevent scarring

### Trachoma
- **Organism**: Chlamydia trachomatis (A, B, Ba, C)
- **Stages**: TF (follicles), TI (intense), TS (scarring), TT (trichiasis), CO (opacity)
- **Clinical**: Herbert's pits, Arlt's line, conjunctival scarring
- **Treatment**: WHO SAFE - Surgery, Antibiotics (azithromycin 1g), Facial cleanliness, Environment
- **SICS**: Leading cause of preventable blindness worldwide

### Pterygium
- **Definition**: Triangular fibrovascular growth encroaching on cornea nasally
- **Etiology**: UV exposure, dust, wind
- **Clinical**: Stocker's line, crosses limbus onto cornea
- **Surgery**: When approaching visual axis (3mm from limbus)
- **Treatment**: Excision with conjunctival autograft. Mitomycin C for recurrence
- **SICS**: Differentiate from pseudopterygium (can pass probe under it)

### Pinguecula
- **Definition**: Yellowish elevated conjunctival degeneration at limbus (doesn't cross)
- **Treatment**: Usually none. Lubricants if inflamed
- **SICS**: Never crosses onto cornea (unlike pterygium)

## 3. CORNEAL DISORDERS

### Bacterial Keratitis
- **Risk**: Contact lens, trauma, post-surgery, dry eyes
- **Clinical**: Suppurative stromal infiltrate with epithelial defect, AC reaction, hypopyon
- **Organisms**: Pseudomonas (CL), S. aureus, S. pneumoniae
- **Investigation**: Corneal scraping - KOH, Gram stain, culture
- **Treatment**: 
  - Fortified: Cefazolin 5% + Tobramycin 1.4% hourly
  - Monotherapy: fluoroquinolones
- **SICS**: Pseudomonas causes rapid progression with ring infiltrate - aggressive treatment

### Fungal Keratitis
- **Risk**: Agricultural trauma, chronic steroid use
- **Clinical**: Feathery margins, satellite lesions, dry texture, endothelial plaque, hypopyon
- **Organisms**: Filamentous (Aspergillus, Fusarium) vs Yeast (Candida)
- **Investigation**: KOH wet mount (hyphae), Sabouraud's agar
- **Treatment**: 
  - Natamycin 5% (filamentous)
  - Amphotericin B 0.15% (yeast)
  - Voriconazole oral for deep infections
- **SICS**: Slower than bacterial. Steroids absolutely contraindicated!

### Viral Keratitis (HSV)
- **Types**: Epithelial (dendritic), Stromal (disciform), Endothelial
- **Clinical**: Dendritic ulcer - linear branching with terminal bulbs, decreased corneal sensation
- **Treatment**: 
  - Acyclovir 3% ointment 5x/day
  - Oral acyclovir for stromal
  - Never steroids in epithelial disease!
- **Recurrence**: Very common. Prophylactic oral acyclovir
- **SICS**: Decreased corneal sensation is key. Geographic ulcer = advanced dendritic

### Corneal Ulcer Workup
- **Symptoms**: Pain, photophobia, lacrimation, decreased vision, FB sensation
- **Signs**: Ciliary congestion, infiltrate, epithelial defect (fluorescein +ve), AC reaction, hypopyon
- **Scraping**: Before antibiotics - KOH, Gram, Giemsa, culture (blood, chocolate, Sabouraud's)
- **Treatment**: Empirical broad-spectrum until culture. Never patch infected ulcers!
- **SICS**: Contact lens = Pseudomonas until proven otherwise

### Corneal Degenerations
- **Arcus Senilis**: Lipid deposit, normal >60yrs. Arcus juvenilis = hyperlipidemia
- **Band Keratopathy**: Calcium in Bowman's (3-9 o'clock). Causes: chronic uveitis, hypercalcemia
- **Salzmann Nodular**: Elevated nodules post-inflammation. Treatment: superficial keratectomy

## 4. GLAUCOMA

### Primary Open Angle Glaucoma (POAG)
- **Definition**: Progressive optic neuropathy, open angles, IOP >21 mmHg
- **Risk**: Age >40, FH, myopia, black race, diabetes, HTN
- **Clinical**: Asymptomatic until late. Cupping (C/D >0.6, vertical elongation, notching)
- **VF**: Nasal step, arcuate scotoma, tunnel vision (late)
- **Target**: Lower IOP 20-50% from baseline
- **Medical**: Prostaglandin (1st) → Beta blockers → Alpha agonists → CAI
- **Surgical**: Trabeculectomy, tube shunts, MIGS
- **SICS**: Silent thief of sight. Screen high risk. Compliance crucial!

### Primary Angle Closure Glaucoma (PACG)
- **Risk**: Hypermetropes, females, Asians, FH
- **Acute Attack**: Severe pain, headache, N/V, halos, rock hard eye
- **Signs**: IOP 40-80 mmHg, corneal edema, mid-dilated pupil, shallow AC, closed angles
- **Emergency Management**:
  1. IV Mannitol 20% 1-2g/kg
  2. IV Acetazolamide 500mg
  3. Topical pilocarpine 2% (after IOP drops)
  4. Topical Timolol
- **Definitive**: Laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) both eyes
- **Chronic**: Insidious, intermittent attacks, PAS formation
- **SICS**: Medical emergency! Vision loss within hours. Always treat fellow eye!

### Normal Tension Glaucoma (NTG)
- **Definition**: POAG with IOP <21 mmHg
- **Pathophysiology**: Vascular insufficiency, low CSF pressure, thin CCT
- **Management**: Same as POAG but target 30% reduction
- **SICS**: Consider in glaucomatous damage with "normal" IOP

### Secondary Glaucomas
- **Inflammatory**: Uveitic - steroids + treat inflammation. PAS common
- **Neovascular**: PDR, CRVO. Rubeosis, ectropion uveae. PRP + anti-VEGF. Refractory needs tube
- **Lens-induced**: 
  - Phacomorphic: intumescent cataract
  - Phacolytic: hypermature cataract
  - Treatment: cataract extraction
- **Traumatic**: Hyphema, angle recession, lens subluxation
- **Steroid-induced**: Prolonged use. Stop steroids, treat like POAG
- **SICS**: Always look for cause. Neovascular has worst prognosis

### Congenital Glaucoma
- **Clinical Triad**: Epiphora, photophobia, blepharospasm
- **Signs**: Buphthalmos, Haab's striae, increased C/D, corneal diameter >12mm
- **Treatment**: Surgical - Goniotomy or Trabeculotomy. Medical inadequate
- **Prognosis**: Good if diagnosed <1 year
- **SICS**: Large eyes or excessive tearing in infant

## 5. CATARACT

### Types & Classification
- **Congenital**: Rubella, galactosemia, Lowe syndrome
- **Developmental**: Presenile (<50 yrs)
- **Senile**: Nuclear sclerosis, cortical, PSC
- **Complicated**: Secondary to uveitis, high myopia, RP
- **Traumatic**: Rosette pattern if penetrating
- **Metabolic**: Diabetes (snowflake), hypoparathyroid, Wilson

### Clinical Features
- **Symptoms**: Painless progressive vision loss, glare, monocular diplopia
- **Nuclear**: Myopic shift, yellow-brown, hard nucleus
- **Cortical**: Spoke-like opacities, water clefts, better in dim light
- **PSC**: Glare, worse in bright light, rapid progression
- **SICS**: PSC most symptomatic despite good distant vision

### Cataract Surgery
- **Indications**: Visual impairment, mature cataract risk, lens-induced glaucoma
- **Preop**: Biometry, A-scan, K-readings, IOL calculation (SRK/T formula)
- **Phacoemulsification**: Small incision (2.8mm), ultrasonic, foldable IOL, faster recovery
- **ECCE**: Large incision (10-12mm), nucleus expression, rigid IOL, for hard cataracts
- **SICS**: Manual small incision (6-7mm), cost-effective
- **Postop**: Prednisolone + moxifloxacin QID, taper over 6 weeks
- **SICS**: Phaco is gold standard. SICS is alternative in developing countries

### Complications
- **Intraop**: PCR, vitreous loss, nucleus drop, suprachoroidal hemorrhage
- **Early**: Corneal edema, endophthalmitis, TASS, wound leak, hyphema
- **Late**: PCO (most common), CME, RD, chronic uveitis
- **Endophthalmitis**: Pain, vision loss, hypopyon, vitreous haze. Emergency: vitreous tap + intravitreal antibiotics (vancomycin + ceftazidime)
- **PCO**: Elschnig pearls, Sommerring ring. Treat: Nd:YAG capsulotomy
- **SICS**: PCO in 20-40% at 2 years. Endophthalmitis 0.1% but devastating

## 6. UVEITIS

### Anterior Uveitis (Iritis)
- **Symptoms**: Pain, photophobia, lacrimation, blurred vision
- **Signs**: Ciliary injection, AC cells & flare, KPs, hypopyon, miosis, posterior synechiae
- **KPs**: Mutton-fat in granulomatous (TB, sarcoid, VKH). Fine in non-granulomatous (HLA-B27, JIA)
- **Associations**: AS (HLA-B27), Reiter, IBD, JIA, Behcet, sarcoid, TB
- **Treatment**: 
  - Cycloplegics (homatropine/atropine) - prevent synechiae
  - Steroids: prednisolone 1% hourly
  - Treat underlying cause
- **SICS**: Always dilate to prevent synechiae! Granulomatous vs non-granulomatous guides workup

### Intermediate Uveitis
- **Definition**: Inflammation of vitreous and peripheral retina
- **Clinical**: Snowballs, snowbanking, CME
- **Associations**: MS, sarcoidosis, Lyme. Often idiopathic (pars planitis)
- **Treatment**: Regional steroids, systemic immunosuppression if bilateral/severe
- **SICS**: Floaters with minimal external signs

### Posterior Uveitis
- **Toxoplasma**: Most common. Focal necrotizing retinitis, "headlight in fog"
  - Treatment: Pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid. Add steroid after 24-48hrs
- **CMV**: HIV/immunocompromised. "Pizza pie" fundus. Treatment: Ganciclovir
- **TB**: Choroidal tubercles, serpiginous-like. Treatment: ATT 9-12 months + steroids
- **VKH**: Bilateral panuveitis, exudative RD, vitiligo, poliosis, alopecia. High-dose steroids
- **SICS**: Always consider infectious causes. Rule out HIV, TB, toxoplasma

### Sympathetic Ophthalmia
- **Definition**: Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis after penetrating injury
- **Timeframe**: 2 weeks to years (80% within 3 months)
- **Clinical**: Dalen-Fuchs nodules, sunset glow fundus
- **Treatment**: High-dose systemic steroids + immunosuppression
- **Prevention**: Primary repair of all penetrating injuries
- **SICS**: Once in both eyes, enucleation doesn't help

## 7. RETINAL DISORDERS

### Diabetic Retinopathy
- **NPDR**: 
  - Mild: microaneurysms only
  - Moderate: hemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton wool spots
  - Severe: 4-2-1 rule (4 quad hemorrhages OR 2 quad venous beading OR 1 quad IRMA)
- **PDR**: NVD/NVE, vitreous hemorrhage, tractional RD
- **DME**: Macular edema, hard exudates in macula
- **Investigation**: FFA, OCT
- **Treatment**:
  - Laser: PRP for severe NPDR/PDR. Focal/grid for DME
  - Anti-VEGF: bevacizumab, ranibizumab, aflibercept for DME and PDR
  - Surgery: vitrectomy for non-clearing VH, tractional RD
- **Screening**: Type 1 at 5yrs, then annual. Type 2 at diagnosis, then annual
- **SICS**: HbA1c <7% crucial. Sudden vision loss = VH or RD

### Hypertensive Retinopathy
- **Grading**:
  - Grade 1: arteriolar narrowing
  - Grade 2: AV nicking
  - Grade 3: hemorrhages, exudates, CWS
  - Grade 4: papilledema
- **Malignant HTN**: Grade 4 with macular star. Medical emergency!
- **Management**: Control BP. Grade 3-4 needs urgent control
- **SICS**: Fundus correlates with systemic complications

### Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)
- **Clinical**: Sudden painless vision loss. "Blood & thunder" fundus
- **Risk**: HTN, DM, glaucoma, hypercoagulable
- **BRVO**: Sector hemorrhages. Better prognosis
- **Ischemic**: Non-perfusion >10 DD, RAPD, poor vision. High neovascular risk
- **Treatment**: Anti-VEGF for macular edema. PRP if neovascularization
- **Workup**: CBC, FBS, lipids, BP, thrombophilia if young
- **SICS**: 90-day glaucoma - neovascular develops ~3 months after ischemic CRVO

### Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)
- **Clinical**: Sudden profound vision loss. Cherry red spot, boxcarring
- **Timing**: Retina survives only 90 minutes
- **Emergency**: Ocular massage, AC paracentesis, carbogen, IV Mannitol, lower IOP
- **Workup**: ESR (GCA if >50), carotid Doppler, echo
- **BRAO**: Altitudinal/sector defect. Better prognosis
- **SICS**: Stroke equivalent of eye. Always rule out GCA

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)
- **Dry**: Drusen, RPE changes, geographic atrophy. Gradual vision loss
- **Wet**: CNV, subretinal fluid/blood, rapid vision loss
- **Diagnosis**: OCT (fluid, PED), FFA (leakage, CNV)
- **Treatment**: Anti-VEGF (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab) monthly initially
- **Home monitoring**: Amsler grid for metamorphopsia
- **Prevention**: AREDS vitamins for intermediate AMD
- **SICS**: Sudden distortion in elderly = wet AMD. Urgent referral!

### Retinal Detachment
- **Types**: Rhegmatogenous (break), Tractional, Exudative
- **RRD Risk**: Myopia, aphakia/pseudophakia, trauma, lattice, FH
- **Symptoms**: Floaters, flashes, shadow/curtain
- **Signs**: Corrugated retina, convex, tobacco dust, breaks
- **Prophylaxis**: Laser/cryo for symptomatic breaks
- **Surgery**: Scleral buckle, pneumatic retinopexy, vitrectomy
- **Prognosis**: Macula-on 90%, Macula-off 40-60%
- **SICS**: Flashes + floaters = PVD → examine for breaks. Urgent if RD!

## 8. NEURO-OPHTHALMOLOGY

### Optic Neuritis
- **Definition**: Inflammation of optic nerve, usually demyelinating
- **Clinical**: Acute painful vision loss (days), worse with movement, RAPD, dyschromatopsia, central scotoma
- **Fundus**: Papillitis (swelling) or retrobulbar (normal disc). "Patient sees nothing, doctor sees nothing"
- **Associations**: MS (50% in 15 years), NMO
- **Investigation**: MRI brain/orbit with contrast
- **Treatment**: IV methylprednisolone 1g/day x3 days. Speeds recovery
- **ONTT**: Oral prednisone alone increases recurrence!
- **Prognosis**: 90% recover to 6/12+
- **SICS**: Young female + painful vision loss + RAPD = ON. MRI for MS risk

### Papilledema
- **Definition**: Disc swelling due to raised ICP
- **Etiology**: Masses, venous thrombosis, IIH, meningitis
- **Clinical**: Bilateral disc swelling, visual obscurations, preserved VA initially, enlarged blind spot
- **Emergency**: Headache, N/V, diplopia (6th palsy) → urgent CT/MRI + LP
- **Differentiation from Papillitis**:
  - Papillitis: unilateral, painful, decreased VA, RAPD, young
  - Papilledema: bilateral, painless, preserved VA initially
- **IIH**: Obese women, bilateral papilledema, normal MRI, high LP pressure
- **IIH Treatment**: Weight loss, acetazolamide, topiramate. ONSF or shunt if failing
- **SICS**: True emergency if acute with neuro signs

### Third Nerve Palsy
- **Complete**: Ptosis, eye down & out, dilated fixed pupil, cannot adduct/elevate/depress
- **Pupil Involved**: SURGICAL - aneurysm, uncal herniation. EMERGENCY! CT angiography
- **Pupil Sparing**: MEDICAL - diabetes, HTN (ischemic). Spares in 80-90%
- **Investigation**: 
  - Pupil involved: urgent CTA/MRA
  - Pupil sparing in >50 with DM/HTN: observe, resolves in 3 months
- **Management**: Treat cause. Surgery after 6 months if no recovery
- **SICS**: Pupil involvement = aneurysm until proven otherwise!

### Horner Syndrome
- **Triad**: Ptosis (mild), miosis, anhidrosis (if preganglionic/central)
- **Clinical**: Dilation lag, upside-down ptosis, iris heterochromia (congenital)
- **Pharmacology**: Cocaine confirms, Apraclonidine reverses, Hydroxyamphetamine localizes
- **Causes**: Pancoast tumor, carotid dissection (painful), cluster headache, iatrogenic, congenital
- **Pediatric**: Neuroblastoma, birth trauma. Always image!
- **SICS**: Painful Horner = carotid dissection → urgent CTA. Pediatric = scan chest/abdomen

### Sixth Nerve Palsy
- **Clinical**: Horizontal diplopia worse on distance, esotropia, limited abduction
- **Causes**: Ischemic (DM, HTN), trauma, raised ICP (false localizing), tumor, MS
- **Investigation**: Isolated in young or non-resolving: MRI
- **Management**: Treat cause. Botox or patch. Surgery at 6 months
- **SICS**: Bilateral = raised ICP. Trauma: skull base fracture

### Myasthenia Gravis
- **Ocular**: Ptosis (variable, worse evening), diplopia (fluctuating), orbicularis weakness
- **Ice Test**: Ice pack → improvement
- **Investigation**: AChR antibodies (50% ocular), MuSK. Repetitive nerve stimulation
- **Tensilon Test**: Edrophonium improves symptoms temporarily
- **CT Chest**: Rule out thymoma
- **Treatment**: 
  - Pyridostigmine
  - Steroids
  - Thymectomy if thymoma
- **SICS**: Variable ptosis that worsens during day. Ice test diagnostic

## 9. OCULAR TRAUMA

### Chemical Injury
- **Alkali**: Worse than acid. Penetrates deeply, liquefactive necrosis
- **Acid**: Coagulative necrosis, self-limiting
- **Emergency Management**:
  1. Immediate copious irrigation (30 minutes minimum)
  2. Check pH (neutral 7.0-7.4)
  3. Remove particulate matter
  4. Don't delay for anesthesia
- **Later**: Cycloplegics, steroids, ascorbate, antibiotics
- **Complications**: Corneal perforation, symblepharon, glaucoma
- **SICS**: Alkali burns (lime, ammonia) are ophthalmic emergencies. Irrigate first, examine later!

### Blunt Trauma
- **Hyphema**: Blood in AC. Grade 1-4 based on height
  - Management: Head elevation, cycloplegics, steroids, IOP control
  - Complications: Rebleed (days 3-5), glaucoma, corneal blood staining
- **Commotio Retinae**: Berlin's edema. Gray-white retinal opacity
- **Traumatic Optic Neuropathy**: Vision loss after trauma. Consider steroids vs observation
- **Globe Rupture**: Decreased vision, shallow AC, peaked pupil, prolapse
  - Never apply pressure
  - Shield the eye
  - Urgent surgical repair
- **SICS**: Any hyphema needs admission. Watch for rebleed days 3-5

### Penetrating Injury
- **Signs**: Prolapsed tissue, shallow AC, irregular pupil, positive Seidel test
- **Management**:
  1. Shield (no pad/pressure)
  2. Systemic antibiotics
  3. Tetanus prophylaxis
  4. Urgent surgical repair within 24 hours
- **IOFB**: X-ray, CT for localization. Remove if accessible
- **Endophthalmitis Risk**: High. Prophylactic intravitreal antibiotics considered
- **SICS**: Never explore wound. Shield and repair urgently. Consider sympathetic ophthalmia risk

### Orbital Fractures
- **Blowout**: Inferior/medial wall. Diplopia (restricted upgaze), enophthalmos, infraorbital anesthesia
- **CT Scan**: Mandatory for diagnosis
- **Indications for Surgery**: 
  - Persistent diplopia >2 weeks
  - Large defect >50%
  - Enophthalmos
- **SICS**: Forced duction test positive. Never blow nose (surgical emphysema)

## 10. PEDIATRIC OPHTHALMOLOGY

### Strabismus
- **Esotropia**: 
  - Infantile: <6 months, alternating, large angle. Surgery
  - Accommodative: 2-3 years, hypermetropia, AC/A ratio. Glasses
- **Exotropia**: Intermittent → constant. Surgery when constant or poor control
- **Vertical**: Superior oblique palsy (head tilt to opposite side)
- **Assessment**: 
  - Cover-uncover test
  - Alternate cover test
  - Hirschberg test
  - Krimsky test
- **SICS**: Always check for amblyopia. Treat amblyopia before surgery

### Amblyopia
- **Definition**: Decreased vision without organic cause, develops <8 years
- **Types**: Strabismic, Anisometropic, Deprivation (ptosis, cataract)
- **Critical Period**: Birth to 8 years
- **Treatment**: 
  - Correct refractive error
  - Occlusion therapy (patch better eye)
  - Penalization (atropine in better eye)
- **Success**: Best if started <6 years
- **SICS**: Screen all children. Treat aggressively before age 8

### Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP)
- **Risk**: <32 weeks, <1500g, oxygen therapy
- **Zones**: I (posterior), II (mid), III (peripheral)
- **Stages**: 1-5 (1=demarcation → 5=total RD)
- **Plus Disease**: Vascular dilation & tortuosity
- **Type 1 ROP** (requires treatment):
  - Zone I: any stage with plus
  - Zone I: stage 3 without plus
  - Zone II: stage 2-3 with plus
- **Screening**: First at 31 weeks or 4 weeks after birth
- **Treatment**: Laser photocoagulation to avascular retina. Anti-VEGF in aggressive posterior
- **SICS**: Screening prevents blindness. Type 1 needs urgent treatment

### Congenital Cataract
- **Causes**: Rubella, genetic, metabolic (galactosemia)
- **Examination**: Red reflex absent, leukocoria
- **Management**:
  - Surgery within 6 weeks if dense bilateral
  - IOL controversial in infants
  - Contact lens or aphakic glasses
  - Treat amblyopia aggressively
- **SICS**: Early surgery crucial for visual development. Amblyopia treatment equally important

### Leukocoria (White Pupil)
- **DDx**: 
  - Retinoblastoma (most important)
  - Congenital cataract
  - ROP
  - PHPV
  - Toxocara
  - Coats disease
- **Investigation**: Fundus exam, USG, CT/MRI
- **SICS**: Always rule out retinoblastoma first!

### Retinoblastoma
- **Genetics**: RB1 gene. Bilateral if germline (40%)
- **Clinical**: Leukocoria, strabismus, red eye
- **Staging**: Intraocular → extraocular → metastatic
- **Investigation**: Fundus, USG (calcification), MRI (no CT - radiation risk)
- **Treatment**:
  - Small: laser, cryotherapy
  - Medium: chemotherapy
  - Large: enucleation
  - Germline: screen siblings & offspring
- **Prognosis**: 95% survival if intraocular
- **SICS**: Leukocoria in child = retinoblastoma until proven otherwise

## 11. IMPORTANT SICS POINTS

### Emergencies - ACT FAST
1. **Acute Angle Closure**: IV Mannitol + Acetazolamide + Pilocarpine + Timolol → LPI
2. **Chemical Injury**: Irrigate 30 min immediately, check pH
3. **CRAO**: Ocular massage, AC paracentesis, carbogen within 90 min
4. **Endophthalmitis**: Vitreous tap + intravitreal antibiotics within hours
5. **Globe Rupture**: Shield, no pressure, repair within 24
