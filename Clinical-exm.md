# Ophthalmology Clinical Examination Notes
## Based on Kanski's Clinical Ophthalmology

---

## HIRSCHBERG TEST

### Purpose
- Quick screening test for strabismus
- Estimates the angle of deviation
- Useful in young children or uncooperative patients who cannot perform cover tests

### Technique
1. Patient fixates on a pen torch held approximately 33 cm away
2. Examiner observes the corneal light reflexes in both eyes
3. Note the position of the light reflex relative to the pupil center

### Interpretation
- **Normal**: Light reflex is centered in both pupils
- **Esotropia (ET)**: Reflex displaced temporally in the deviating eye
- **Exotropia (XT)**: Reflex displaced nasally in the deviating eye
- **Hypertropia**: Reflex displaced inferiorly in the deviating eye
- **Hypotropia**: Reflex displaced superiorly in the deviating eye

### Angle Estimation
- **1 mm displacement** = approximately **15 prism diopters** of deviation
- Reflex at pupil margin = 15Δ
- Reflex midway between pupil center and limbus = 30Δ
- Reflex at limbus = 45Δ

## DIRECT CONFRONTATION TEST (Cover Test)

### Purpose
- Detects manifest strabismus (tropia)
- More accurate than Hirschberg test
- Reveals the deviating eye

### Technique
1. Patient fixates on a distant target (6 meters) with both eyes open
2. Cover one eye and observe the uncovered eye
3. If the uncovered eye moves to take up fixation, a manifest deviation is present
4. Repeat for the other eye
5. Test at near (33 cm) as well

### Interpretation of Eye Movement

**Uncovered eye moves:**
- **Outward (temporally)** → Esotropia was present
- **Inward (nasally)** → Exotropia was present
- **Downward** → Hypertropia was present
- **Upward** → Hypotropia was present

**No movement** → No manifest strabismus in that eye (or the covered eye was already the deviating eye)

---

## COVER-UNCOVER TEST

### Purpose
- Differentiates between tropia (manifest) and phoria (latent) deviations
- Detects heterotropia and heterophoria

### Technique
1. Patient fixates on a target (distance and near)
2. Cover one eye and observe that eye under the cover
3. Remove the cover and observe the same eye for movement
4. Repeat for the other eye

### Interpretation

**Movement on covering (manifest deviation - TROPIA):**
- Eye under cover deviates from fixation
- Movement on uncovering indicates the eye was deviated

**Movement only on uncovering (latent deviation - PHORIA):**
- Eye remains straight under cover
- Movement when uncovered shows the eye drifted when dissociated

**Direction of movement on uncovering:**
- **Outward** → Esophoria
- **Inward** → Exophoria
- **Downward** → Hyperphoria
- **Upward** → Hypophoria

---

## ALTERNATE COVER TEST (PRISM COVER TEST)

### Purpose
- Measures the total deviation (manifest + latent)
- Most accurate method for quantifying strabismus
- Used with prisms to neutralize the deviation

### Technique
1. Patient fixates on a target
2. Alternately cover each eye, moving the occluder rapidly between eyes
3. Do not allow binocular vision to be restored
4. Observe for refixation movements

### With Prisms (Quantification)
1. Perform alternate cover test
2. Place prisms of increasing strength before one eye
3. Continue until no refixation movement is seen
4. The prism strength that neutralizes movement = angle of deviation

### Interpretation
- This test breaks fusion completely
- Measures the maximum angle of deviation
- Useful for surgical planning
- Should be performed at distance (6m) and near (33cm)

---

## OCULAR MOTILITY TESTING

### Purpose
- Assess extraocular muscle function
- Detect muscle paresis or restriction
- Evaluate the pattern of strabismus

### Technique - Versions (Binocular Movements)

**Patient follows a target in 9 diagnostic positions:**
1. Primary position (straight ahead)
2. Right gaze
3. Left gaze
4. Up gaze
5. Down gaze
6. Up and right
7. Up and left
8. Down and right
9. Down and left

**Observe for:**
- Smoothness of pursuit
- Range of movement
- Nystagmus
- Overshoot or undershoot

---

## TYPES OF EYE MOVEMENTS

### 1. DUCTIONS (Monocular Movements)
Movement of ONE eye with the other eye covered

**Types:**
- **Abduction**: Movement temporally (away from nose)
- **Adduction**: Movement nasally (toward nose)
- **Elevation (Supraduction)**: Upward movement
- **Depression (Infraduction)**: Downward movement

**Grading:**
- 0 = No movement
- -1 = Severe limitation (up to 25% of normal)
- -2 = Moderate limitation (25-50%)
- -3 = Mild limitation (50-75%)
- -4 = Slight limitation (75-90%)
- Normal = Full range

---

### 2. VERSIONS (Binocular Conjugate Movements)
Movement of BOTH eyes in the SAME direction simultaneously

**Types:**
- **Dextroversion**: Both eyes move right
- **Levoversion**: Both eyes move left
- **Supraversion (Elevation)**: Both eyes move up
- **Infraversion (Depression)**: Both eyes move down
- **Dextro-elevation**: Both eyes up and right
- **Dextro-depression**: Both eyes down and right
- **Levo-elevation**: Both eyes up and left
- **Levo-depression**: Both eyes down and left

---

### 3. VERGENCES (Binocular Disconjugate Movements)
Movement of both eyes in OPPOSITE directions

**Types:**
- **Convergence**: Both eyes move inward (nasally)
  - Tested by asking patient to fixate on approaching object
  - Normal: Convergence to within 5-10 cm of nose
  - Break point = when one eye deviates outward

- **Divergence**: Both eyes move outward (temporally)
  - Less commonly tested
  - Occurs when looking from near to distance

---

### 4. CYCLODUCTIONS/TORSIONS (Rotational Movements)
Rotation of the eye around the anteroposterior axis

**Types:**
- **Intorsion (Incycloduction)**: Top of eye rotates nasally
- **Extorsion (Excycloduction)**: Top of eye rotates temporally

**Tested by:**
- Double Maddox rod test
- Fundus photography showing disc position
- Observation of iris/conjunctival vessels

**Clinical relevance:**
- Superior oblique palsy causes extorsion
- Can cause cyclotorsional diplopia (tilted images)

---

## MUSCLES IN DIAGNOSTIC POSITIONS

### Primary Action of Each Muscle in Diagnostic Gaze Positions

**Right eye looking:**
- **Right and up** → Right superior rectus (RSR)
- **Right and down** → Right inferior rectus (RIR)
- **Left and up** → Right inferior oblique (RIO)
- **Left and down** → Right superior oblique (RSO)
- **Right** → Right lateral rectus (RLR)
- **Left** → Right medial rectus (RMR)

**Left eye looking:**
- **Left and up** → Left superior rectus (LSR)
- **Left and down** → Left inferior rectus (LIR)
- **Right and up** → Left inferior oblique (LIO)
- **Right and down** → Left superior oblique (LSO)
- **Left** → Left lateral rectus (LLR)
- **Right** → Left medial rectus (LMR)

---

## CLINICAL PEARLS

1. **Hirschberg test**: Quick screening, estimates angle, useful in children
2. **Cover test**: Differentiates tropia from phoria, essential in every eye exam
3. **Alternate cover test**: Measures total deviation, best for quantification
4. **Always test at distance AND near**: Some deviations vary with fixation distance
5. **Version testing**: Identifies which muscle is weak or restricted
6. **Convergence**: Important for assessing near work ability, reading problems

---

## COMMON PATTERNS

### Esotropia
- Eyes turn inward
- Hirschberg: Temporal displacement of reflex
- Cover test: Uncovered eye moves outward

### Exotropia
- Eyes turn outward
- Hirschberg: Nasal displacement of reflex
- Cover test: Uncovered eye moves inward

### Hypertropia
- One eye higher than the other
- Often due to vertical muscle imbalance
- Check for head tilt (superior oblique palsy)

# RETINOSCOPY

## Definition
Objective method of determining refractive error by observing the movement of light reflected from the retina

## Principle
- Light is projected into the eye using a retinoscope
- The reflex from the retina is observed through the pupil
- Movement of the reflex indicates the refractive state
- Lenses are added to neutralize the reflex movement

## Types of Retinoscopes

### 1. Streak Retinoscope (Most Common)
- Projects a linear streak of light
- Can be rotated to align with meridians
- Better for detecting astigmatism

### 2. Spot Retinoscope
- Projects a circular spot of light
- Easier for beginners
- Less precise for astigmatism

## Working Distance
- Standard: **66 cm (0.66 m)** = +1.50 D working distance lens
- Alternative: **50 cm (0.5 m)** = +2.00 D working distance lens
- The working distance lens must be subtracted from final result

## Retinoscopic Reflexes

### WITH Movement
- Reflex moves in the SAME direction as the streak
- Indicates: **Myopia less than working distance** OR **Hypermetropia**
- Action: Add PLUS (+) lenses

### AGAINST Movement
- Reflex moves in OPPOSITE direction to the streak
- Indicates: **Myopia greater than working distance**
- Action: Add MINUS (-) lenses

### NEUTRALIZATION
- No movement of reflex
- Reflex fills entire pupil uniformly
- Bright, clear reflex
- Endpoint reached

## Technique

### Preparation
1. Dim room lighting
2. Patient removes spectacles
3. Patient fixates on distant target (to relax accommodation)
4. Examiner sits at working distance (66 cm)
5. Start with patient's right eye, examiner uses right eye

### Steps
1. **Shine light into pupil** - Observe reflex
2. **Sweep the streak** across pupil horizontally and vertically
3. **Note direction** of reflex movement
4. **Add lenses** to trial frame or phoropter:
   - WITH movement → Add PLUS lenses
   - AGAINST movement → Add MINUS lenses
5. **Neutralize** in each meridian (0° and 90°)
6. **Subtract working distance** from final result

### For Astigmatism
1. Rotate streak to find principal meridians
2. Neutralize each meridian separately
3. The two meridians will require different lens powers
4. Difference = cylindrical component

## Characteristics of Reflex

| Feature | Myopia (High) | Low Ametropia | Hypermetropia (High) |
|---------|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Brightness | Dim | Bright | Very bright |
| Speed | Slow | Fast | Very fast |
| Width | Narrow | Wide | Very wide |
| Movement | Against | Against/With | With |

## Interpretation

### Example 1: Simple Myopia
- Reflex: Against movement in all meridians
- Neutralization: -3.00 D
- Working distance: +1.50 D (at 66 cm)
- **Final Rx: -3.00 - (+1.50) = -4.50 D sphere**

### Example 2: Simple Hypermetropia
- Reflex: With movement in all meridians
- Neutralization: +3.50 D
- Working distance: +1.50 D
- **Final Rx: +3.50 - (+1.50) = +2.00 D sphere**

### Example 3: Compound Myopic Astigmatism
- Horizontal (180°): Neutralizes at -2.00 D
- Vertical (90°): Neutralizes at -4.00 D
- Working distance: +1.50 D
- **Final Rx: -3.50 -2.00 × 180** OR **-5.50 +2.00 × 90**

## Common Errors
1. Incorrect working distance
2. Accommodation in young patients (use cycloplegia)
3. Poor fixation
4. Off-axis examination
5. Parallax error

# OPTICS & REFRACTIVE ERRORS

## BASIC OPTICAL PRINCIPLES

### Refraction
Bending of light as it passes from one medium to another of different refractive index

### Refractive Index (n)
- Speed of light in vacuum / Speed of light in medium
- Air: n = 1.00
- Cornea: n = 1.376
- Aqueous/Vitreous: n = 1.336
- Lens: n = 1.386-1.406

### Snell's Law
n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
- i = angle of incidence
- r = angle of refraction

### Focal Length (f)
Distance from lens to focal point where parallel rays converge

### Lens Power (P)
- Measured in **Diopters (D)**
- P = 1/f (where f is in meters)
- Example: f = 0.5 m → P = 1/0.5 = +2.00 D

### Types of Lenses

**Convex (Plus/Positive) Lenses:**
- Converging lenses
- Thicker in center
- Used for hypermetropia
- Form real, inverted images (when object beyond focal point)

**Concave (Minus/Negative) Lenses:**
- Diverging lenses
- Thinner in center
- Used for myopia
- Form virtual, erect, diminished images

---

## EMMETROPIA

### Definition
State of refraction where parallel rays of light are focused exactly on the retina with accommodation relaxed

### Characteristics
- No refractive error
- Clear distance vision without correction
- Typical eye:
  - Axial length: ~23-24 mm
  - Total refractive power: ~60 D
  - Corneal power: ~43 D
  - Lens power: ~17 D (relaxed)

---

## AMETROPIA (REFRACTIVE ERRORS)

## 1. MYOPIA (Near-sightedness)

### Definition
Light rays focus ANTERIOR to the retina with accommodation relaxed

### Types

**A. Axial Myopia** (Most Common - 95%)
- Excessive axial length (>24 mm)
- Normal corneal curvature
- Each 1 mm increase = ~3 D myopia

**B. Refractive/Curvature Myopia**
- Excessive corneal curvature
- Normal axial length
- Seen in keratoconus, lenticonus

**C. Index Myopia**
- Increased refractive index of lens
- Nuclear sclerosis (early cataract)
- Diabetes (sorbitol accumulation)

**D. Positional Myopia**
- Anterior displacement of lens
- Seen in Marfan syndrome, ectopia lentis

### Classification by Degree
- **Low myopia**: < -3.00 D
- **Moderate myopia**: -3.00 to -6.00 D
- **High myopia**: > -6.00 D
- **Pathological myopia**: > -6.00 D with degenerative changes

### Clinical Features
- **Symptoms:**
  - Blurred distance vision
  - Clear near vision
  - Squinting
  - Headaches
  - Eye strain

- **Signs:**
  - Negative spherical lenses improve vision
  - Large optic disc
  - Temporal crescent
  - Tessellated fundus (high myopia)

### Complications (High Myopia)
1. **Retinal detachment** (10× risk)
2. **Posterior vitreous detachment**
3. **Lattice degeneration**
4. **Myopic macular degeneration**
5. **Lacquer cracks (Bruch's membrane breaks)**
6. **Fuchs' spot** (CNV at macula)
7. **Open-angle glaucoma** (increased risk)
8. **Posterior staphyloma**
9. **Cataract** (earlier onset)

### Management
- **Optical correction:**
  - Concave (minus) lenses
  - Contact lenses
  - For -3.00 D myopia, prescribe -3.00 D lens
  
- **Refractive surgery:**
  - LASIK
  - PRK
  - SMILE
  - Phakic IOL (high myopia)

- **Prevention/Control (in children):**
  - Atropine eye drops (0.01%)
  - Outdoor activity (2 hours/day)
  - Orthokeratology
  - Multifocal contact lenses

---

## 2. HYPERMETROPIA (Far-sightedness)

### Definition
Light rays focus POSTERIOR to the retina with accommodation relaxed

### Types

**A. Axial Hypermetropia** (Most Common)
- Short axial length (<23 mm)
- Normal corneal curvature
- Each 1 mm decrease = ~3 D hypermetropia

**B. Refractive/Curvature Hypermetropia**
- Flat cornea (low curvature)
- Normal axial length

**C. Index Hypermetropia**
- Decreased refractive index of lens
- Aphakia
- Old age (lens protein changes)

**D. Positional Hypermetropia**
- Posterior displacement of lens
- Rare

### Classification

**By Degree:**
- **Low**: < +2.00 D
- **Moderate**: +2.00 to +5.00 D
- **High**: > +5.00 D

**By Manifestation:**
1. **Total Hypermetropia** = Latent + Manifest

2. **Latent Hypermetropia**
   - Corrected by ciliary tone
   - Revealed only after cycloplegia
   - Usually ~1.00 D in adults

3. **Manifest Hypermetropia** = Facultative + Absolute
   - **Facultative**: Corrected by accommodation
   - **Absolute**: Cannot be corrected by accommodation, needs glasses

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms:**
- Blurred near vision (more than distance)
- Eyestrain, asthenopia
- Headaches (frontal, after near work)
- Letters "run together" when reading
- Intermittent blurred distance vision (young patients)

**In Children:**
- May cause accommodative esotropia
- Amblyopia (if unilateral high hypermetropia)

**Signs:**
- Small optic disc
- Crowded disc (pseudopapilledema)
- Positive lenses improve vision
- Shallow anterior chamber

### Complications
1. **Accommodative esotropia** (most common in children)
2. **Amblyopia** (if anisometropia)
3. **Angle-closure glaucoma** (shallow AC, crowded angle)
4. **Chronic angle-closure glaucoma**
5. **Asthenopia**

### Management
- **Optical correction:**
  - Convex (plus) lenses
  - May need cycloplegia to determine full hypermetropia
  - Under-correct slightly in young patients (leave 0.50-1.00 D)
  
- **Children with accommodative esotropia:**
  - Full hypermetropic correction
  - Bifocals if high AC/A ratio

- **Refractive surgery:**
  - LASIK (low to moderate)
  - Clear lens extraction + IOL (high hypermetropia)

## 3. ASTIGMATISM

### Definition
Refractive error where light rays do not focus at a single point due to different refractive powers in different meridians

### Cause
- Usually corneal (irregular curvature)
- Less commonly lenticular
- Corneal astigmatism: Most common due to greater curvature in vertical meridian

### Types by Axis

**A. With-the-Rule (WTR) Astigmatism**
- Vertical meridian (90°) has greater power than horizontal (180°)
- Minus cylinder axis at 180° ± 20°
- Plus cylinder axis at 90° ± 20°
- Most common in young people

**B. Against-the-Rule (ATR) Astigmatism**
- Horizontal meridian (180°) has greater power than vertical (90°)
- Minus cylinder axis at 90° ± 20°
- Plus cylinder axis at 180° ± 20°
- Common in elderly (corneal flattening with age)

**C. Oblique Astigmatism**
- Principal meridians at oblique axes (30-60° or 120-150°)
- More symptomatic
- Harder to adapt to correction

### Types by Refraction

**1. Regular Astigmatism**
Two principal meridians perpendicular to each other

**A. Simple Astigmatism:**
- One meridian emmetropic, other ametropic
- **Simple myopic**: One meridian focused on retina, other in front
- **Simple hypermetropic**: One meridian on retina, other behind

**B. Compound Astigmatism:**
- Both meridians ametropic (same type)
- **Compound myopic**: Both meridians focus in front of retina
- **Compound hypermetropic**: Both meridians focus behind retina

**C. Mixed Astigmatism:**
- One meridian myopic, other hypermetropic
- One focuses in front, one behind retina
- Circle of least confusion falls on retina

**2. Irregular Astigmatism**
- Principal meridians not perpendicular
- Meridian power varies across it
- Cannot be fully corrected with spectacles
- Causes:
  - Keratoconus
  - Corneal scarring
  - Pterygium
  - Lenticular irregularities
- Corrected with rigid contact lenses or corneal transplant

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms:**
- Blurred vision (distance and/or near)
- Asthenopia
- Eye strain
- Headaches
- Tilting head to improve vision
- Squinting

**Signs:**
- Retinoscopy shows different neutralization in different meridians
- Pinhole improves vision in regular astigmatism
- Stenopaic slit at appropriate axis improves vision

### Notation

**Example 1:** -2.00 -1.00 × 180
- Sphere: -2.00 D
- Cylinder: -1.00 D (negative cylinder form)
- Axis: 180° (horizontal meridian)

**Example 2:** +1.00 +2.00 × 90
- Sphere: +1.00 D
- Cylinder: +2.00 D (positive cylinder form)
- Axis: 90° (vertical meridian)

### Converting Between Forms

**Negative to Positive Cylinder:**
-2.00 -1.00 × 180 = -3.00 +1.00 × 90

**Rule:**
1. New sphere = Old sphere + Old cylinder
2. New cylinder = Old cylinder (change sign)
3. New axis = Old axis ± 90°

### Management
- **Regular astigmatism:**
  - Cylindrical lenses (spectacles)
  - Toric contact lenses
  - Refractive surgery (LASIK, PRK)
  
- **Irregular astigmatism:**
  - Rigid gas-permeable contact lenses
  - Scleral lenses
  - Corneal transplant (severe cases)
  - Topography-guided LASIK

## 4. ANISOMETROPIA

### Definition
Difference in refractive error between the two eyes

### Classification
- **Significant**: Difference ≥ 1.00 D
- **Mild**: 1.00-2.00 D difference
- **Moderate**: 2.00-3.00 D difference
- **Severe**: > 3.00 D difference

### Problems
1. **Aniseikonia** - Different image sizes
   - 1% aniseikonia per 1.00 D difference in spectacles
   - Causes: diplopia, asthenopia, suppression
   
2. **Amblyopia** - In children (more hypermetropic/myopic eye)

3. **Binocular vision problems**
   - Difficulty fusing images
   - Suppression of one eye

### Management
- **Contact lenses** (preferred - less aniseikonia)
- **Spectacles** (if difference < 3.00 D)
- **Refractive surgery**
- **Treat amblyopia early** in children

## 5. PRESBYOPIA

### Definition
Progressive loss of accommodation due to aging, resulting in difficulty with near vision

### Pathophysiology
- Lens loses elasticity with age
- Decreased zonular tension effect
- Ciliary muscle changes
- Amplitude of accommodation decreases

### Age of Onset
- Typically begins: 40-45 years
- Functionally significant: 45-50 years
- Earlier in:
  - Hypermetropes (45 years)
  - Uncorrected myopes notice later (48-50 years)
  
### Amplitude of Accommodation

| Age (years) | Amplitude (D) | Near Point (cm) |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| 10 | 14.0 | 7 |
| 20 | 10.0 | 10 |
| 30 | 7.0 | 14 |
| 40 | 4.5 | 22 |
| 45 | 3.5 | 28 |
| 50 | 2.5 | 40 |
| 60 | 1.0 | 100 |

**Hofstetter's Formula:**
- Maximum amplitude = 25 - (0.4 × age)
- Minimum amplitude = 15 - (0.25 × age)

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms:**
- Difficulty reading small print
- Need to hold reading material farther away
- Eyestrain during near work
- Headaches after reading
- Need for brighter light for reading
- Symptoms worse at end of day

### Near Addition Calculation

**Working distance** = 40 cm (typical reading distance)
**Required power** = 100/40 = 2.50 D

**Add formula:** Add = Required power - Available accommodation

**Example 1:** 45-year-old emmetrope
- Available accommodation ≈ 3.50 D
- Required for 40 cm = 2.50 D
- **Add = 2.50 - 3.50/2 = +1.00 to +1.25 D**

**Example 2:** 50-year-old emmetrope
- Available accommodation ≈ 2.50 D
- **Add = 2.50 - 1.25 = +1.25 to +1.50 D**

**General Guidelines:**
- Age 40-45: +0.75 to +1.00 D
- Age 45-50: +1.25 to +1.50 D
- Age 50-55: +1.75 to +2.00 D
- Age 55-60: +2.25 to +2.50 D
- Age 60+: +2.50 to +3.00 D

### Management Options

**1. Reading Glasses**
- Plus lenses for near work only
- Simple, inexpensive
- Patient must remove for distance

**2. Bifocals**
- Distance correction in upper segment
- Near addition in lower segment
- Line visible between segments
- Types: Flat-top, Executive, Round segment

**3. Trifocals**
- Three segments: Distance, Intermediate, Near
- Useful for computer work
- Two visible lines

**4. Progressive (Varifocal) Lenses**
- Gradual power change from top to bottom
- No visible lines
- Aesthetic advantage
- Peripheral distortion in corridor
- Adaptation period needed

**5. Contact Lenses**
- Monovision: One eye for distance, other for near
- Multifocal contact lenses
- Alternating near and distance zones

**6. Surgical Options**
- Monovision LASIK
- Multifocal IOLs (cataract surgery)
- Corneal inlays (KAMRA)
- Refractive lens exchange

## ACCOMMODATION

### Definition
Ability of the eye to increase its refractive power to focus on near objects

### Mechanism
1. Parasympathetic stimulation (CN III)
2. Ciliary muscle contraction
3. Zonules relax
4. Lens becomes more convex (especially anterior surface)
5. Increased lens power (~10-12 D in young)

### Near Triad
Accommodation is accompanied by:
1. **Accommodation** - Lens power increase
2. **Convergence** - Eyes turn inward
3. **Miosis** - Pupil constriction

### Accommodation Amplitude
- Maximum power lens can add to focus on near objects
- Measured in diopters (D)
- Decreases with age (presbyopia)

**Measurement:**
- Push-up method
- Minus lens method
- Accommodative rule

### AC/A Ratio
Accommodative Convergence / Accommodation ratio
- Normal: 3-5:1
- High AC/A: Excessive convergence for accommodation
  - Causes esotropia at near
  - Treat with bifocals
- Low AC/A: Insufficient convergence
  - Causes exotropia at near

## PRESCRIBING PRINCIPLES

### General Rules

**Myopia:**
- Prescribe full correction for adults
- May under-correct slightly in children (0.25-0.50 D)
- Progressive myopia: Update frequently

**Hypermetropia:**
- Young adults: Under-correct (leave 0.50-1.00 D)
- Older adults: Near full correction
- Children with esotropia: Full cycloplegic correction
- Asymptomatic low hypermetropia (<+1.00 D): May not need correction

**Astigmatism:**
- Correct > 0.75 D in adults
- Correct > 0.50 D in children
- New oblique astigmatism: Start with partial correction
- Full correction for regular astigmatism once adapted

**First-time presbyopes:**
- Start with minimum add that provides comfort
- +0.75 to +1.00 D initially
- Increase gradually as symptoms worsen

### Spectacle Tolerance

**Maximum changes tolerated:**
- Sphere: ±0.50 to ±1.00 D
- Cylinder: ±0.50 D (power and axis)
- Axis: ±5-10° (if cylinder > 0.75 D)

**Red flags for intolerance:**
- Large change in axis (>15°)
- Change from WTR to ATR
- First-time oblique astigmatism
- Anisometropia > 2.00 D in new patient
