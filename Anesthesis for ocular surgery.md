# Anesthesia for Ocular Surgery - Complete Notes

## INTRODUCTION

Ophthalmic anesthesia aims to:
- Provide adequate analgesia (pain control)
- Achieve akinesia (immobility of eye and eyelids)
- Minimize intraocular pressure (IOP)
- Ensure patient comfort and cooperation
- Provide safe surgical conditions
- Minimize complications

---

## GENERAL CONSIDERATIONS

### Factors Influencing Choice of Anesthesia

**Patient Factors:**
- Age (children, elderly)
- Medical comorbidities (cardiac, respiratory, bleeding disorders)
- Mental state (anxiety, cognitive impairment, claustrophobia)
- Ability to lie flat and remain still
- Patient preference
- Language barriers/deafness

**Surgical Factors:**
- Type of surgery (anterior vs posterior segment)
- Expected duration
- Complexity of procedure
- Need for akinesia
- Surgeon preference and experience

**Ocular Factors:**
- Axial length (high myopia - increased risk with blocks)
- Previous surgery
- Scleral integrity
- Open globe injury (contraindication to regional blocks)

## TYPES OF ANESTHESIA

### 1. TOPICAL ANESTHESIA

**Definition:** Surface anesthesia using topical drops/gel without injection

**Agents:**
- **Proparacaine 0.5%** (most commonly used)
- **Tetracaine 0.5%** (amethocaine)
- **Lidocaine 2% or 4% gel**
- **Oxybuprocaine 0.4%**
- **Benoxinate**

**Mechanism:** Blocks nerve conduction in corneal and conjunctival nerve endings

**Technique:**
1. Instill drops 2-3 times at 5-minute intervals before surgery
2. Additional drops during surgery as needed
3. May add intracameral lidocaine (preservative-free 1% lidocaine in anterior chamber)

**Advantages:**
- Rapid onset (30-60 seconds)
- No needle-related complications
- No risk of globe perforation, hemorrhage, or muscle injury
- Preserves vision immediately post-op
- Early visual rehabilitation
- No chemosis or subconjunctival hemorrhage
- Cost-effective
- Suitable for day-care surgery
- Can be used in anticoagulated patients

**Disadvantages:**
- No akinesia (eye movements possible)
- Requires patient cooperation
- Patient may see instruments during surgery (psychologically disturbing)
- Surgeon needs to be experienced
- Not suitable for anxious/uncooperative patients
- Limited analgesia duration
- Potential for corneal toxicity with repeated use
- Risk of posterior capsule rupture if patient moves

**Indications:**
- **Phacoemulsification** (most common use)
- **Trabeculectomy**
- **Simple pterygium surgery**
- **Minor anterior segment procedures**
- Clear corneal cataract surgery

**Contraindications:**
- Uncooperative/anxious patients
- Confused/demented patients
- Communication difficulties (deafness, language barrier)
- Tremor or inability to lie still
- Chronic cough
- Claustrophobia
- Long/complicated surgery anticipated
- Surgeon inexperienced with topical anesthesia

**Complications:**
- Corneal epithelial toxicity (if overused)
- Allergic reactions (rare)
- Patient discomfort if inadequate

---

### 2. SUB-TENON'S (PARABULBAR) ANESTHESIA

**Definition:** Injection of local anesthetic into sub-Tenon's space (between Tenon's capsule and sclera)

**Anatomy:** 
- Tenon's capsule envelops globe from limbus to optic nerve
- Sub-Tenon's space is potential space between Tenon's and sclera
- Contains fine connective tissue septa

**Technique:**

**A. Anterior (Limbal) Approach** (Most Common):
1. Topical anesthesia to conjunctiva
2. Small conjunctival and Tenon's incision (3-5mm) in inferonasal quadrant, 3-5mm from limbus
3. Blunt curved cannula inserted through incision into sub-Tenon's space
4. Cannula advanced posteriorly along globe curvature (10-15mm)
5. 3-5ml local anesthetic injected slowly
6. Gentle massage to distribute anesthetic

**B. Posterior Approach:**
- Similar technique but incision made more posteriorly
- Used less commonly

**Anesthetic Agents:**
- **Lidocaine 2%** (4ml) + **Bupivacaine 0.5%** (1ml)
- Or **Ropivacaine 0.75-1%**
- May add **hyaluronidase** (increases spread)
- Usually NO epinephrine needed

**Advantages:**
- Excellent analgesia
- Good akinesia (not as complete as peribulbar/retrobulbar)
- Lower risk than sharp needle techniques
- No risk of globe perforation (blunt cannula)
- Reduced risk of retrobulbar hemorrhage
- Can be used in high myopes and long eyes
- Lower volume of anesthetic needed
- Rapid onset (5-7 minutes)
- Suitable for most anterior and posterior segment surgeries

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires surgical dissection
- Small conjunctival/Tenon's incision needed
- Possible subconjunctival hemorrhage (usually minimal)
- Chemosis can occur
- Incomplete akinesia in some cases
- Technically more demanding than topical

**Indications:**
- **Cataract surgery** (alternative to topical in anxious patients)
- **Vitreoretinal surgery**
- **Glaucoma surgery**
- Patients unsuitable for peribulbar/retrobulbar (high myopia, anticoagulation)

**Contraindications:**
- Active infection
- Previous extensive conjunctival surgery (scarring)
- Open globe injury

**Complications:**
- **Subconjunctival hemorrhage** (most common, usually minimal)
- Chemosis
- Incomplete block (may need supplementation)
- Injury to extraocular muscles (rare with blunt cannula)
- Scleral perforation (very rare)

### 3. PERIBULBAR ANESTHESIA

**Definition:** Injection of local anesthetic into orbit outside muscle cone

**Anatomy:**
- Anesthetic deposited in extraconal space
- Diffuses around muscle cone to reach retrobulbar space
- Does not enter muscle cone directly (unlike retrobulbar)

**Technique:**

**Standard Two-Injection Technique:**

**Inferotemporal Injection:**
1. Needle insertion at junction of lateral 1/3 and medial 2/3 of lower lid
2. Tangential to globe, directed slightly backward and toward orbital apex
3. Needle advanced 25-30mm (but not to full depth)
4. After negative aspiration, inject 5-8ml slowly

**Superomedial/Nasal Injection:**
1. Needle insertion at medial canthus or superonasal quadrant
2. Directed upward and backward
3. 3-5ml injected after negative aspiration

**Single-Injection Technique** (Modified):
- Single inferotemporal injection with larger volume (8-10ml)
- Medial needle angulation to facilitate spread

**Needles:**
- 25mm or 32mm, 25-gauge needle
- Blunt or sharp (blunt safer)

**Anesthetic Agents:**
- **Lidocaine 2%** (5-7ml) + **Bupivacaine 0.5%** (3-5ml)
- Total volume: 8-12ml
- **Hyaluronidase** 15-30 IU/ml (enhances spread)
- Usually without epinephrine

**Onset:** 10-15 minutes

**Advantages:**
- Excellent analgesia
- Good akinesia
- **Safer than retrobulbar** (lower risk of serious complications)
- No globe perforation risk if technique correct
- Lower risk of optic nerve injury
- Lower risk of brainstem anesthesia
- Suitable for most intraocular surgeries

**Disadvantages:**
- Larger volume needed
- Chemosis and lid swelling
- Longer onset time than retrobulbar
- Risk of orbital hemorrhage
- May cause ptosis
- Requires experience

**Indications:**
- **Cataract surgery** (when topical/sub-Tenon's inadequate)
- **Vitreoretinal surgery**
- **Long/complex anterior segment procedures**
- Anxious/uncooperative patients unsuitable for topical
- Surgeon preference for complete akinesia

**Contraindications:**
- Inability to lie flat
- Severe coagulopathy
- Open globe injury
- Previous extensive orbital surgery
- Patient refusal

**Complications:**

**Minor:**
- Subconjunctival/lid hemorrhage (common, usually self-limiting)
- Chemosis
- Lid ecchymosis
- Transient diplopia
- Ptosis (temporary)

**Major (Rare):**
- **Retrobulbar hemorrhage** (0.1-3%)
  - Presents as proptosis, increasing IOP, pain
  - Management: Immediate canthotomy/cantholysis if severe
- **Globe perforation** (0.01-0.13%)
  - Risk higher in high myopia, long eyes, staphyloma
- **Optic nerve injury** (very rare)
- **Brainstem anesthesia** (extremely rare, 0.001%)
  - Spread via optic nerve sheath
  - Presents with loss of consciousness, respiratory depression, bradycardia
  - EMERGENCY - requires resuscitation
- Extraocular muscle injury
- Central retinal artery occlusion (very rare)

### 4. RETROBULBAR ANESTHESIA

**Definition:** Injection of local anesthetic directly into retrobulbar space (within muscle cone)

**Anatomy:**
- Muscle cone formed by four rectus muscles
- Retrobulbar space contains: optic nerve, ciliary ganglion, nerves, vessels

**Technique:**
1. Needle insertion at junction of lateral 1/3 and medial 2/3 of lower lid (similar to peribulbar)
2. Initial direction tangential to globe (first 15mm)
3. Then redirect upward and medially toward orbital apex
4. Advance needle to about 30-35mm depth
5. **Aspiration test mandatory** (check for vascular puncture)
6. If negative, inject 3-4ml slowly

**Needles:** 35mm, 23-25 gauge, sharp

**Anesthetic Agents:**
- **Lidocaine 2%** (2ml) + **Bupivacaine 0.5%** (2ml)
- Total volume: 3-5ml (less than peribulbar)
- Hyaluronidase may be added

**Onset:** 5-10 minutes (faster than peribulbar)

**Advantages:**
- Excellent, profound anesthesia
- Rapid onset
- Complete akinesia
- Smaller volume required
- Effective blockade of all nerves

**Disadvantages:**
- **Higher complication rate than peribulbar**
- Risk of serious sight-threatening and life-threatening complications
- Requires precise technique
- Sharp needle increases perforation risk

**Indications:**
- **Now largely replaced by peribulbar/sub-Tenon's**
- May still be used for specific complex procedures
- When rapid onset essential

**Contraindications:**
- High myopia (axial length >26mm)
- Previous scleral buckling
- Posterior staphyloma
- Coagulopathy
- Only seeing eye (relative)

**Complications:**

**All complications of peribulbar PLUS:**
- **Higher risk of globe perforation** (especially in high myopes)
- **Optic nerve damage** (direct trauma or vascular compromise)
- **Optic nerve sheath injection** → CSF spread → brainstem anesthesia
- **Central retinal artery occlusion**
- **Retrobulbar hemorrhage** (higher risk)
- Subarachnoid injection
- **Brainstem anesthesia** (higher risk than peribulbar)

**Signs of Brainstem Anesthesia:**
- Confusion, disorientation
- Loss of consciousness
- Respiratory depression/arrest
- Cardiovascular collapse
- Contralateral cranial nerve palsies
- Shivering
- Usually occurs within 5-10 minutes

**Management:** Immediate resuscitation, airway support, cardiovascular support

### 5. FACIAL NERVE BLOCK (O'Brien/Van Lint/Nadbath Block)

**Purpose:** To achieve **orbicularis oculi akinesia** (prevent squeezing of eyelids)

**Not routinely used anymore** due to:
- Risk of facial nerve palsy
- Lid hematoma
- Modern techniques (sub-Tenon's, peribulbar) often provide adequate lid akinesia

**Techniques:**

**A. O'Brien Block:**
- Injection over condyle of mandible
- Blocks facial nerve trunk before branching

**B. Van Lint Block:**
- Subcutaneous injection lateral to lateral canthus
- Blocks peripheral branches of facial nerve

**C. Nadbath Block:**
- Behind ear, blocks facial nerve at stylomastoid foramen
- Higher complication risk

**Modern Alternative:** Intermittent manual lid pressure or Honan balloon

### 6. GENERAL ANESTHESIA (GA)

**Definition:** Complete loss of consciousness with loss of protective reflexes

**Indications:**

**Patient-Related:**
- **Children** (primary indication)
- Mentally challenged/uncooperative adults
- Severe anxiety/phobia (cannot tolerate local)
- Claustrophobia
- Deaf-blind patients
- Severe tremor/Parkinson's disease
- Chronic cough, unable to lie flat

**Surgery-Related:**
- **Penetrating eye injury** (open globe) - ABSOLUTE indication
- Long, complex surgery (>2 hours)
- Surgery requiring complete akinesia
- Surgery on both eyes simultaneously
- Extensive orbital/oculoplastic surgery

**Failed Regional Block:**
- Supplementation not possible

**Advantages:**
- Complete patient comfort
- Perfect akinesia
- Airway control
- Suitable for all ages
- No risk of needle-related complications
- Ideal for penetrating trauma (no pressure on globe)

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher risk than local anesthesia
- Requires anesthesiologist
- More expensive
- Longer recovery time
- Risks of GA (aspiration, cardiovascular events)
- Post-op nausea/vomiting (PONV)
- Delayed visual rehabilitation
- Not suitable for day-care in some patients

**Anesthetic Technique:**

**Pre-medication:**
- Anxiolytic if needed (midazolam)
- Antiemetic prophylaxis (ondansetron)
- Avoid anticholinergics (increase IOP)

**Induction:**
- **Intravenous agents:** Propofol, thiopental
- Smooth induction to avoid coughing (increases IOP)

**Airway Management:**
- **Laryngeal Mask Airway (LMA)** - preferred for routine cases
- **Endotracheal intubation** - for long surgery, aspiration risk
- Avoid straining/bucking (increases IOP)

**Maintenance:**
- Volatile agents: Sevoflurane, isoflurane
- Or TIVA (total intravenous anesthesia) with propofol
- Maintain normocapnia (avoid hypercarbia - increases IOP)
- Muscle relaxants if intubated

**Analgesia:**
- Opioids (fentanyl, remifentanil)
- Multimodal analgesia
- Consider peribulbar/sub-Tenon's block for post-op pain

**IOP Control:**
- Avoid hypoxia, hypercarbia, straining
- Smooth extubation
- Adequate depth of anesthesia

**Special Considerations in Pediatric Ophthalmic Anesthesia:**
- Oculocardiac reflex more common (see below)
- Higher incidence of PONV
- Malignant hyperthermia risk assessment
- Parental anxiety management

## SPECIAL ANESTHETIC CONSIDERATIONS IN OPHTHALMOLOGY

### 1. OCULOCARDIAC REFLEX (OCR)

**Definition:** Trigeminovagal reflex causing bradycardia in response to ocular stimulation

**Mechanism:**
- **Afferent:** Ophthalmic division of trigeminal nerve (V1)
- **Central:** Trigeminal nucleus → vagal nucleus
- **Efferent:** Vagus nerve (X) → heart

**Triggers:**
- Traction on extraocular muscles (especially medial rectus)
- Pressure on globe
- Retrobulbar injection
- Ocular trauma
- Pain

**Clinical Manifestations:**
- **Bradycardia** (most common) - >10% decrease in heart rate
- Other arrhythmias: AV block, asystole (rare)
- Hypotension
- Apnea

**More Common in:**
- Children
- Squint surgery
- Light anesthesia
- Hypoxia, hypercarbia

**Management:**

**Prevention:**
- Adequate depth of anesthesia
- Pre-medication with anticholinergics (controversial)
- Gentle surgical technique
- Avoid hypoxia/hypercarbia

**Treatment:**
1. **Inform surgeon** → Stop surgical manipulation
2. Usually self-limiting within 10-20 seconds
3. If persistent/severe:
   - Give **atropine 0.01-0.02 mg/kg IV** (or glycopyrrolate)
   - Ensure adequate oxygenation
   - Deepen anesthesia if light

**Fatigue Phenomenon:** OCR diminishes with repeated stimulation

### 2. INTRAOCULAR PRESSURE (IOP) CONTROL

**Normal IOP:** 10-21 mmHg

**Factors Increasing IOP:**
- Hypoxia, hypercarbia
- Hypertension
- Coughing, straining, bucking
- Laryngoscopy and intubation
- Suxamethonium (succinylcholine) - increases IOP by 5-10 mmHg
- External pressure on globe
- Valsalva maneuver
- Steep Trendelenburg position

**Factors Decreasing IOP:**
- Most IV induction agents (propofol, thiopental)
- Most volatile agents (except desflurane)
- Non-depolarizing muscle relaxants
- Acetazolamide
- Adequate anesthetic depth
- Controlled ventilation (avoid hypercarbia)

**Critical in:**
- Open globe injury (avoid IOP increase - risk of expulsive hemorrhage)
- Glaucoma surgery
- Penetrating keratoplasty

**Suxamethonium (Succinylcholine) Controversy:**
- **Increases IOP** transiently (5-10 mmHg for 5-10 minutes)
- Mechanism: Tonic contraction of extraocular muscles
- **Absolutely contraindicated** in open globe injury
- Alternative: Rocuronium for rapid sequence induction

---

### 3. POST-OPERATIVE NAUSEA AND VOMITING (PONV)

**High Incidence in Ophthalmic Surgery:**
- Squint surgery (up to 60-80% in children)
- Vitreoretinal surgery
- Oculocardiac reflex
- Manipulation of extraocular muscles

**Risk Factors:**
- Female gender
- Non-smoker
- History of PONV/motion sickness
- Use of volatile agents and opioids
- Duration of surgery

**Consequences:**
- Patient distress
- Risk of wound dehiscence (especially in penetrating keratoplasty)
- Increased IOP
- Suprachoroidal hemorrhage risk

**Prevention:**
- **Multimodal approach:**
  - **5-HT3 antagonists:** Ondansetron 4-8mg IV, granisetron
  - **Dexamethasone** 4-8mg IV (at induction)
  - **Droperidol** (low dose)
  - **Aprepitant** (NK-1 antagonist) for high-risk
- Avoid nitrous oxide
- Propofol-based anesthesia (TIVA)
- Adequate hydration
- Minimize opioid use (use multimodal analgesia)

**Treatment:**
- Ondansetron 4mg IV
- Promethazine
- Metoclopramide

---

### 4. MALIGNANT HYPERTHERMIA (MH)

**Relevance:** Can be triggered by volatile anesthetics and suxamethonium

**Signs:**
- Unexplained tachycardia
- Rapidly rising temperature
- Muscle rigidity
- Hypercarbia (increased end-tidal CO2)
- Metabolic acidosis

**Management:**
- Stop triggering agents immediately
- Give **dantrolene** 2.5 mg/kg IV (repeat as needed)
- Hyperventilate with 100% O2
- Active cooling
- Treat arrhythmias, hyperkalemia
- ICU monitoring

---

### 5. OPEN GLOBE INJURY - ANESTHETIC CONSIDERATIONS

**Primary Goal:** Prevent expulsion of intraocular contents

**Key Principles:**
- **Avoid any increase in IOP**
- Smooth induction and intubation
- **No suxamethonium** (increases IOP)
- Consider **rapid sequence induction with rocuronium** if full stomach
- Gentle airway manipulation
- Adequate depth before laryngoscopy
- Eye shield/protection
- Antiemetic prophylaxis (prevent vomiting)

**Dilemma:** Full stomach (trauma) vs need to avoid suxamethonium

**Options:**
- Rocuronium 1-1.2 mg/kg for rapid onset
- Sugammadex available for reversal if needed
- Or modified rapid sequence with remifentanil

## COMPLICATIONS OF OCULAR ANESTHESIA

### Local/Regional Anesthesia Complications

**Common (Minor):**
1. **Subconjunctival hemorrhage** - most common, benign
2. **Chemosis** - conjunctival edema
3. **Lid ecchymosis and swelling**
4. **Ptosis** - usually temporary
5. **Inadequate block** - may need supplementation or conversion to GA

**Uncommon (Moderate):**
6. **Diplopia** - due to muscle trauma/anesthetic myotoxicity
7. **Corneal abrasion**
8. **Anterior segment ischemia** (rare)

**Rare (Serious):**
9. **Globe perforation** (0.01-0.13%)
   - Higher risk: High myopia, long eyes, posterior staphyloma, previous scleral surgery
   - Symptoms: Sudden pain, loss of red reflex, vitreous hemorrhage
   - Management: Immediate ophthalmology consultation, may need vitrectomy
   
10. **Retrobulbar hemorrhage** (0.1-3%)
    - Symptoms: Proptosis, increasing IOP, pain, ecchymosis
    - Management: Lateral canthotomy/cantholysis if severe, IOP-lowering measures, delay surgery
    
11. **Optic nerve damage**
    - Direct trauma or vascular compromise
    - May result in permanent vision loss
    
12. **Brainstem anesthesia** (0.001%)
    - Due to spread via optic nerve sheath to CSF
    - Symptoms: Confusion, drowsiness, respiratory depression, cardiovascular collapse, contralateral signs
    - Onset: 5-10 minutes post-injection
    - Management: Airway support, ventilation, cardiovascular support, ICU care
    - Recovery usually complete in 2-4 hours
    
13. **Central retinal artery occlusion**
    - Due to increased orbital pressure or direct vascular injury
    - Results in permanent blindness
    
14. **Intravascular injection**
    - Can cause seizures, arrhythmias
    - Prevention: Always aspirate before injection

**Allergic Reactions:**
- True allergy to local anesthetics rare
- More common with ester-type (rarely used now)
- Amide-type (lidocaine, bupivacaine) very rarely allergic
- May be reaction to preservatives

### General Anesthesia Complications
- Aspiration
- Cardiovascular events
- Respiratory complications
- Awareness (rare with modern monitoring)
- Malignant hyperthermia (very rare)

## FACTORS AFFECTING CHOICE OF ANESTHESIA

### Patient Factors
| Factor | Preferred Anesthesia |
|--------|---------------------|
| Cooperative adult | Topical or sub-Tenon's |
| Anxious adult | Sub-Tenon's or peribulbar |
| Child | General anesthesia |
| Anticoagulated | Topical or sub-Tenon's (avoid sharp needles) |
| High myope (AL >26mm) | Topical or sub-Tenon's (avoid retrobulbar) |
| Severe systemic disease | Local (avoid GA risk) |
| Claustrophobic | General anesthesia |
| Only seeing eye | General anesthesia (some prefer local in experienced hands) |

### Surgical Factors
| Surgery Type | Usual Anesthesia |
|--------------|------------------|
| **Phacoemulsification** | Topical ± intracameral |
| **ECCE** | Sub-Tenon's or peribulbar |
| **Trabeculectomy** | Topical or sub-Tenon's |
| **Vitreoretinal surgery** | Sub-Tenon's, peribulbar, or GA |
| **Strabismus surgery** | General anesthesia (especially children) |
| **Oculoplastics** | Local with sedation or GA |
| **Penetrating keratoplasty** | Sub-Tenon's, peribulbar, or GA |
| **Open globe repair** | **General anesthesia** (ABSOLUTE) |

## MONITORING DURING OCULAR ANESTHESIA

### During Local/Regional Anesthesia
- **Continuous verbal contact** with patient
- Pulse oximetry (SpO2)
- Non-invasive blood pressure
- ECG (especially in elderly, cardiac patients)
- Supplemental oxygen via nasal prongs or face mask
- Consciousness level

### During General Anesthesia
- Standard ASA monitoring:
  - ECG
  - Non-invasive BP
  - SpO2
  - End-tidal CO2 (capnography)
  - Temperature
  - Neuromuscular monitoring (if muscle relaxants used)
- BIS monitoring (awareness prevention) if indicated

---

## PRE-OPERATIVE ASSESSMENT FOR ANESTHESIA

### History
- Previous anesthetic experience and complications
- Drug allergies
- Current medications (especially anticoagulants, α-blockers)
- Systemic diseases (diabetes, hypertension, cardiac, respiratory)
- Smoking, alcohol
- Fasting status
- Ability to lie flat
- Anxiety/claustrophobia

### Examination
- General condition, vital signs
- Airway assessment (Mallampati score)
- Cardiovascular and respiratory examination
- Neurological status

### Investigations
- As indicated by systemic status
- Not routinely needed for healthy patients undergoing local anesthesia

### ASA Physical Status Classification
- **ASA I:** Healthy patient
- **ASA II:** Mild systemic disease
- **ASA III:** Severe systemic disease
- **ASA IV:** Severe disease, constant threat to life
- **ASA V:** Moribund, not expected to survive

---

## POST-OPERATIVE CARE

### After Local Anesthesia
- Monitor vital signs until stable
- Eye pad and shield
- Oral analgesia if needed (paracetamol usually sufficient)
- Antiemetic if nausea
- Instructions about post-op medications
- Usually discharged within 1-2 hours
- Must have responsible adult escort

### After General Anesthesia
- Recovery room monitoring until fully awake
- Analgesia as needed
- Antiemetic prophylaxis/treatment
- Longer observation (minimum 2-4 hours)
- Check for PONV, pain, vision
- Discharge criteria met

### Discharge Criteria
- Stable vital signs
- Fully conscious and oriented
- Able to walk (unless contraindicated)
- Minimal nausea/vomiting
- Adequate pain control
- Responsible adult escort
- Written post-op instructions given
- Emergency contact provided

## SPECIAL SITUATIONS

### 1. Anticoagulated Patients

**Assessment:**
- Type of anticoagulant (warfarin, DOACs, aspirin, clopidogrel)
- INR if on warfarin
- Reason for anticoagulation (risk of stopping vs bleeding risk)

**Management:**
- **Topical anesthesia:** Safe, no issue
- **Sub-Tenon's with blunt cannula:** Generally safe
- **Peribulbar/retrobulbar with sharp needle:** Higher bleeding risk
  - Aspirin alone: Usually continue
  - Dual antiplatelet: Consider risk-benefit, discuss with physician
  - Warfarin: If INR <3, can proceed; if >3, higher risk
  - DOACs: Ideally omit morning dose

**Never stop anticoagulation without consulting prescribing physician**

### 2. Diabetic Patients

**Issues:**
- Variable blood sugar control
- Autonomic neuropathy
- Silent ischemia
- Delayed gastric emptying

**Management:**
- Morning list preferable
- Check blood sugar pre-op
- Usually omit morning oral hypoglycemics
- Insulin: Modified regimen (e.g., half usual dose)
- IV dextrose if needed
- Monitor blood sugar intra-op and post-op

### 3. Patients on α-Blockers (Tamsulosin)

**Issue:** Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome (IFIS)

**Anesthetic Considerations:**
- Inform surgeon pre-operatively
- May need longer surgery time
- Surgeon may use iris hooks, Malyugin ring
- Intracameral epinephrine may be used
- Anesthesia choice not affected, but ensure patient can cooperate if topical used

## KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

1. **Most common anesthesia for phacoemulsification:** Topical ± intracameral lidocaine

2. **Safest regional block technique:** Sub-Tenon's (lowest complication rate)

3. **Peribulbar vs Retrobulbar:** Peribulbar safer, now preferred over retrobulbar

4. **Absolute indication for GA:** Open globe injury

5. **Oculocardiac reflex:** Most common in children during squint surgery; managed by stopping stimulation

6. **Suxamethonium:** Increases IOP; **contraindicated in open globe**

7. **Most common complication of regional block:** Subconjunctival hemorrhage (benign)

8. **Most serious complication of regional block:** Brainstem anesthesia (rare but life-threatening)

9. **Highest risk for globe perforation:** High myopes with retrobulbar/peribulbar block

10. **PONV prevention:** Multimodal approach (ondansetron + dexamethasone, avoid N2O, use propofol)

11. **Anticoagulated patients:** Safe with topical or sub-Tenon's; higher risk with sharp needle blocks

12. **Volume of anesthetic:** 
    - Retrobulbar: 3-5ml
    - Peribulbar: 8-12ml  
    - Sub-Tenon's: 3-5ml

13. **Onset time:**
    - Topical: 30-60 seconds
    - Retrobulbar: 5-10 minutes
    - Peribulbar: 10-15 minutes
    - Sub-Tenon's: 5-7 minutes

14. **Modern trend:** Moving away from sharp needle techniques (retrobulbar, peribulbar) toward topical and sub-Tenon's anesthesia

15. **Patient communication:** Essential throughout surgery under local anesthesia for monitoring and safety
