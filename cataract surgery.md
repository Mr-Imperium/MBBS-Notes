# Cataract Surgery: Types and Procedures
## Based on Khurana and Kanski

## TYPES OF CATARACT SURGERY

### 1. INTRACAPSULAR CATARACT EXTRACTION (ICCE)

**Definition**: Removal of entire lens with intact capsule

**Indications**:
- Subluxated/dislocated lens
- Lens with weak zonules (pseudoexfoliation, Marfan syndrome)
- Hard cataract in elderly patients
- When posterior capsule rupture has occurred in ECCE

**Procedure**:
1. Peritomy and hemostasis
2. Superior rectus bridle suture
3. Large corneoscleral incision (160-180°)
4. Peripheral iridectomy
5. Alpha-chymotrypsin injection (to lyse zonules)
6. Cryoprobe application to lens
7. Gentle tumbling and delivery of lens
8. Alpha-chymotrypsin washout
9. Suturing of wound

**Complications**:
- Vitreous loss (most common)
- Aphakic bullous keratopathy
- Cystoid macular edema
- Retinal detachment
- Expulsive hemorrhage

**Advantages**:
- No posterior capsule opacification
- Single procedure removes entire lens

**Disadvantages**:
- Large incision
- More complications
- Loss of capsular support for IOL
- Requires general anesthesia usually

---

### 2. EXTRACAPSULAR CATARACT EXTRACTION (ECCE)

**Definition**: Removal of nucleus and cortex leaving posterior capsule intact

**Indications**:
- Most cataracts
- Younger patients
- When phacoemulsification not available
- Very hard cataracts in developing countries

**Types**:
- **Conventional ECCE**: Manual expression
- **Small Incision Cataract Surgery (SICS)**: Manual small incision technique

#### Conventional ECCE Procedure:

1. **Incision**: Large corneoscleral or scleral tunnel (10-12 mm)
2. **Anterior capsulotomy**: Can opener or continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis (CCC)
3. **Hydrodissection**: Separation of cortex from capsule
4. **Nucleus delivery**: Expression through wound
5. **Cortex removal**: Irrigation-aspiration
6. **IOL implantation**: In capsular bag or sulcus
7. **Wound closure**: Multiple sutures

#### Small Incision Cataract Surgery (SICS):

**Features**:
- 6-7 mm scleral tunnel incision
- Self-sealing valve incision
- Manual nucleus extraction
- Faster visual rehabilitation than conventional ECCE
- Less expensive than phacoemulsification

**Procedure**:
1. Scleral tunnel construction (V-shaped or straight)
2. Internal corneal valve incision
3. Anterior capsulotomy or capsulorhexis
4. Hydroprocedures (hydrodissection, hydrodelineation)
5. Nucleus prolapse into anterior chamber
6. Nucleus delivery (viscoexpression or using instruments)
7. Cortical aspiration
8. IOL implantation
9. Wound self-sealing (usually no sutures needed)

**Advantages of ECCE**:
- Preserves posterior capsule (supports IOL)
- Lower risk of retinal detachment vs ICCE
- Can implant posterior chamber IOL
- Less vitreous loss

**Complications**:
- Posterior capsule opacification (20-50%)
- Endophthalmitis
- Cystoid macular edema
- Induced astigmatism (with large incision)
- Wound leak

### 3. PHACOEMULSIFICATION

**Definition**: Ultrasonic fragmentation and aspiration of lens nucleus through small incision

**Indications**:
- Most cataracts (soft to moderately hard)
- Patient preference for rapid recovery
- When advanced technology available

**Contraindications**:
- Very hard cataract (may cause complications)
- Brunescent/black cataract
- Shallow anterior chamber
- Corneal endothelial compromise
- Zonular weakness

#### Procedure:

**1. Incision Construction**:
- Clear corneal or scleral tunnel (2.2-3.2 mm)
- Side port incisions for second instrument

**2. Anterior Capsulotomy**:
- Continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis (CCC)
- Size: 5-5.5 mm diameter
- Centered on visual axis

**3. Hydroprocedures**:
- **Hydrodissection**: Fluid wave under capsule to separate cortex
- **Hydrodelineation**: Separates nucleus into epinucleus and endonucleus

**4. Nucleus Emulsification**:

**Techniques**:

a. **Divide and Conquer**:
   - Sculpt deep grooves in nucleus
   - Crack nucleus into quadrants
   - Emulsify each quadrant

b. **Phaco Chop**:
   - Horizontal chopping with second instrument
   - Less ultrasound energy
   - Good for hard cataracts
   - Requires skill

c. **Stop and Chop**:
   - Initial groove + chopping
   - Combination technique

d. **Prechop/Prechopping**:
   - Mechanical chopping before phaco probe
   - Minimal ultrasound usage

**5. Cortex Removal**:
- Irrigation-aspiration system
- Bimanual or coaxial technique

**6. IOL Implantation**:
- Foldable IOL through small incision
- In-the-bag placement preferred
- Viscoelastic removal

**7. Wound Hydration**:
- Self-sealing, usually no sutures

#### Phacoemulsification Settings:

**Power Modulations**:
- Continuous mode
- Pulse mode
- Burst mode
- Hyperpulse/Micropulse

**Fluidics**:
- Flow rate: 20-40 cc/min
- Aspiration: varies by technique
- Bottle height: controls IOP

**Advantages**:
- Small incision (minimal astigmatism)
- Rapid visual recovery
- Low complication rate
- Sutureless
- Same-day surgery
- Better wound integrity

**Complications**:
- Posterior capsule rupture (most common)
- Corneal endothelial damage
- Corneal burns (from ultrasound)
- Zonular dialysis
- Nucleus drop into vitreous
- Iris prolapse
- Descemet's membrane detachment
- Toxic anterior segment syndrome (TASS)

---

### 4. FEMTOSECOND LASER-ASSISTED CATARACT SURGERY (FLACS)

**Definition**: Automated cataract surgery using femtosecond laser technology

**Laser Functions**:
1. Corneal incisions (main and side ports)
2. Anterior capsulotomy (precise circular opening)
3. Lens fragmentation (softens nucleus)
4. Arcuate incisions (for astigmatism correction)

**Advantages**:
- Precise capsulotomy (better IOL centration)
- Reduced ultrasound energy
- Reproducible incisions
- Can correct astigmatism simultaneously

**Disadvantages**:
- Expensive
- Longer procedure time
- Requires patient docking
- Not suitable for all cases (miotic pupils, corneal opacities)

## INTRAOCULAR LENS (IOL) IMPLANTATION

### IOL Types:

**By Material**:
- PMMA (rigid)
- Acrylic (foldable, hydrophobic/hydrophilic)
- Silicone (foldable)

**By Design**:
- Monofocal
- Multifocal
- Toric (for astigmatism)
- Accommodating
- Extended depth of focus (EDOF)

**By Position**:
- Anterior chamber IOL (ACIOL)
- Iris-fixated IOL
- Posterior chamber IOL (PCIOL) - most common
  - In-the-bag
  - Sulcus-fixated
  - Scleral-fixated

### IOL Power Calculation:

**Formulas**:
- **3rd Generation**: SRK/T, Holladay, Hoffer Q
- **4th Generation**: Haigis, Barrett Universal II
- **For post-refractive surgery**: Special formulas needed

**Measurements Required**:
- Axial length (optical biometry preferred)
- Keratometry
- Anterior chamber depth
- Lens thickness (newer formulas)

**Target Refraction**:
- Emmetropia for distance vision
- Mild myopia for near vision (monovision)

## SPECIAL SITUATIONS

### 1. Pediatric Cataract Surgery

**Considerations**:
- Primary posterior capsulotomy + anterior vitrectomy
- Risk of amblyopia
- Aggressive postoperative inflammation
- IOL calculation challenging (growth)
- May leave aphakic initially (age <2 years controversial)

### 2. Complicated Cataracts

**Weak Zonules**:
- Capsular tension ring (CTR)
- Modified CTR with scleral fixation
- Gentle technique

**Hard Cataracts**:
- SICS may be preferred
- Phaco-chop technique
- Higher energy settings
- Risk of zonular damage

**White Cataract**:
- Trypan blue for capsule staining
- Careful capsulotomy
- Risk of capsular rupture (liquefied cortex)

### 3. Combined Procedures

- **Cataract + Glaucoma**: Trabeculectomy with cataract extraction
- **Cataract + Vitrectomy**: Triple procedure
- **Cataract + Keratoplasty**: Combined surgery in corneal disease

## POSTOPERATIVE CARE

### Medications:
1. **Topical antibiotics**: 1 week (e.g., moxifloxacin)
2. **Topical steroids**: Tapering over 4-6 weeks (e.g., prednisolone acetate)
3. **NSAIDs**: To prevent CME (e.g., nepafenac)
4. **Cycloplegics**: Short-term if needed

### Follow-up Schedule:
- Day 1
- Week 1
- Month 1
- Month 3 (final refraction and glasses)

### Expected Visual Recovery:
- **Phacoemulsification**: 1-2 days to weeks
- **ECCE/SICS**: 2-6 weeks
- **ICCE**: 6-12 weeks

## COMPLICATIONS OF CATARACT SURGERY

### Intraoperative:
1. Posterior capsule rupture (1-3%)
2. Zonular dialysis
3. Suprachoroidal hemorrhage
4. Nucleus drop
5. Iridodialysis
6. Descemet's membrane detachment

### Early Postoperative (days to weeks):
1. Endophthalmitis (0.1%)
2. Toxic anterior segment syndrome (TASS)
3. Wound leak
4. Iris prolapse
5. Hypotony
6. IOP spike
7. Corneal edema
8. Hyphema

### Late Postoperative (weeks to years):
1. **Posterior capsule opacification (PCO)** - most common
   - Treatment: Nd:YAG laser capsulotomy
2. Cystoid macular edema (Irvine-Gass syndrome)
3. Retinal detachment
4. IOL dislocation
5. Chronic endophthalmitis
6. Bullous keratopathy
7. Glaucoma

## POSTERIOR CAPSULE OPACIFICATION (PCO)

**Incidence**: 20-50% by 5 years (varies by IOL type)

**Pathophysiology**: Proliferation of residual lens epithelial cells

**Risk Factors**:
- Younger age
- Uveitis
- Diabetes mellitus
- Myotonic dystrophy
- IOL material and design

**Treatment**: Nd:YAG Laser Posterior Capsulotomy
- **Procedure**: Create opening in posterior capsule
- **Energy**: 1-4 mJ per shot
- **Complications**: 
  - IOP rise (treat prophylactically)
  - Retinal detachment (1-3%)
  - CME
  - IOL damage/pitting
  - Anterior hyaloid rupture

**Prevention**:
- Meticulous cortex removal
- IOL with square edge design
- In-the-bag IOL placement

## COMPARATIVE OUTCOMES

| Feature | ICCE | ECCE | SICS | Phaco |
|---------|------|------|------|-------|
| Incision size | 12-14 mm | 10-12 mm | 6-7 mm | 2-3 mm |
| Anesthesia | GA/Retrobulbar | Peribulbar/Topical | Peribulbar/Topical | Topical |
| Surgery time | 30-45 min | 30-45 min | 15-20 min | 10-15 min |
| Visual recovery | Slow | Moderate | Faster | Fastest |
| Astigmatism | High | Moderate | Low | Minimal |
| PCO | None | 20-50% | 20-50% | 15-30% |
| Cost | Low | Low | Low | High |
| Technology | Minimal | Minimal | Minimal | Advanced |

---

## KEY POINTS

1. **Phacoemulsification** is the gold standard in developed countries
2. **SICS** is excellent for high-volume surgery in resource-limited settings
3. **Posterior capsule preservation** is critical for IOL support and reducing complications
4. **IOL implantation** has revolutionized cataract surgery outcomes
5. **PCO** remains the most common long-term complication
6. **Continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis (CCC)** is essential for safe modern cataract surgery
7. Proper **patient selection** and **surgical technique** minimize complications
