# Cataract: Morphological and Etiological Classification & Pre-operative Procedures

## MORPHOLOGICAL CLASSIFICATION OF CATARACT

### Based on Maturity
**1. Immature Cataract**
- Partially opaque lens with some clear areas
- Iris shadow present on oblique illumination
- Patient has some useful vision
- Can see fundus details on ophthalmoscopy

**2. Mature Cataract**
- Entire lens cortex is opaque
- No iris shadow on oblique illumination
- Vision reduced to PL (perception of light) or HM (hand movements)
- Cannot see fundus - absent red reflex

**3. Hypermature Cataract**
- Advanced stage of mature cataract
- Two subtypes:
  - **Morgagnian cataract**: Liquefied cortex with brown nucleus settling inferiorly, giving "bag of water" appearance
  - **Shrunken cataract**: Wrinkled, shrunken capsule with calcium deposits, reduced lens size

**4. Intumescent Cataract**
- Swollen lens due to water imbibition
- Increased lens thickness
- Shallow anterior chamber
- Risk of angle closure glaucoma (phacomorphic glaucoma)

### Based on Anatomical Location

**1. Cortical Cataract**
- Affects lens cortex
- Subtypes: cuneiform (wedge-shaped), cupuliform (saucer-shaped)
- Usually age-related

**2. Nuclear Cataract**
- Affects lens nucleus
- Progressive nuclear sclerosis with browning (brunescent) or black (nigra) coloration
- Common in elderly
- Causes myopic shift ("second sight")

**3. Subcapsular Cataract**
- **Posterior subcapsular (PSC)**: Most visually significant, causes glare, more symptomatic in bright light
- **Anterior subcapsular**: Less common, associated with trauma or inflammation

**4. Polar Cataract**
- Located at anterior or posterior pole
- Often congenital
- May be stationary or progressive

**5. Lamellar (Zonular) Cataract**
- Affects specific layer of lens
- Common congenital type
- Riders (club-shaped opacities) may be present

## ETIOLOGICAL CLASSIFICATION OF CATARACT

### 1. Age-Related (Senile) Cataract
- Most common type (>90% of cases)
- Usually bilateral but asymmetric
- Multifactorial: oxidative stress, UV exposure, protein denaturation
- Types: cortical, nuclear, PSC or mixed

### 2. Congenital Cataract
**Causes:**
- Intrauterine infections: TORCH (Toxoplasma, Rubella, CMV, HSV)
- Metabolic: galactosemia, galactokinase deficiency, Lowe syndrome
- Chromosomal: Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Turner syndrome
- Hereditary: autosomal dominant most common
- Idiopathic

**Morphological types:** lamellar, polar, total, sutural, membranous

### 3. Traumatic Cataract
- Penetrating injury: rosette cataract, total cataract
- Blunt trauma: Vossius ring (iris pigment imprint on anterior lens capsule)
- Electric shock, lightning, radiation
- May develop immediately or years later

### 4. Metabolic Cataract
- **Diabetic cataract**: True diabetic (snowflake cataract in young diabetics with poor control), accelerated senile changes
- **Hypocalcemia**: Hypoparathyroidism, causes subcapsular opacities with iridescent crystals
- **Wilson disease**: Sunflower cataract (copper deposition)
- **Myotonic dystrophy**: Christmas tree cataract (polychromatic crystals)

### 5. Complicated Cataract
Secondary to intraocular disease:
- Chronic anterior uveitis: posterior subcapsular, bread crumb appearance
- Retinitis pigmentosa
- High myopia
- Retinal detachment
- Glaucoma

### 6. Drug-Induced Cataract
- **Corticosteroids**: Posterior subcapsular cataract (most common drug-induced)
- **Phenothiazines**: Anterior subcapsular stellate opacities
- **Amiodarone**: Whorl-like deposits
- **Miotics** (long-term use)

### 7. Radiation Cataract
- Ionizing radiation: posterior subcapsular
- Infrared radiation: glassblower's cataract (exfoliation at anterior capsule)
- UV radiation: cortical cataract

### 8. Toxic Cataract
- Naphthalene poisoning
- Ergot poisoning
- Dinitrophenol

## PRE-OPERATIVE PROCEDURES FOR CATARACT SURGERY

### A. PRE-OPERATIVE ASSESSMENT

#### 1. History Taking
- Chief complaints: blurred vision, glare, difficulty with ADLs
- Duration and progression of symptoms
- Medical history: diabetes, hypertension, cardiac disease, bleeding disorders, medications (anticoagulants, α-blockers like tamsulosin - risk of floppy iris syndrome)
- Ocular history: previous surgery, trauma, uveitis
- Allergies
- Social history: occupation, visual needs, ability to comply with post-op care

#### 2. Ocular Examination

**Visual Acuity**
- Best corrected visual acuity (BCVA) - distance and near
- Pinhole vision
- Potential acuity testing if view of fundus is poor

**External Examination**
- Eyelids: blepharitis, ectropion, entropion (requires correction first)
- Lacrimal system: check for dacryocystitis (contraindication - treat first)
- Conjunctiva: infection, pterygium

**Slit Lamp Examination**
- Cornea: clarity, dystrophies, scars, endothelial status (specular microscopy if needed)
- Anterior chamber: depth (risk of angle closure), cells/flare
- Iris: synechiae, pseudo-exfoliation, pupil size and dilation
- Lens: type of cataract, posterior capsule status, zonular integrity

**Pupillary Examination**
- RAPD (relative afferent pupillary defect) - indicates optic nerve pathology
- Pupil dilation - if poor, may need iris hooks or pupil expansion devices

**Intraocular Pressure**
- Rule out glaucoma
- Pre-existing glaucoma may affect surgical planning

**Fundus Examination**
- Dilated fundoscopy: retinal pathology, macular status, disc evaluation
- If view is poor: B-scan ultrasonography to rule out retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, tumors

**Special Investigations**
- **Biometry** (most important pre-op investigation):
  - Optical biometry (IOLMaster, Lenstar) - preferred, more accurate
  - A-scan ultrasound biometry - if optical biometry not possible
  - Measures: axial length, anterior chamber depth, lens thickness, keratometry
  - IOL power calculation using formulas: SRK-T, Haigis, Barrett, Hoffer Q (selection depends on axial length)

- **Keratometry**: Corneal curvature measurement

- **B-scan ultrasonography**: If fundus not visible, to assess posterior segment

- **Specular microscopy**: Endothelial cell count (especially in Fuchs' dystrophy, previous surgery)

- **Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)**: Macular pathology assessment if fundus visible

- **Potential Acuity Meter (PAM)** or **Laser Interferometry**: Predict post-operative visual outcome when view of retina is obscured

### B. SYSTEMIC EVALUATION

#### 1. Medical Clearance
- General physical examination
- Blood pressure measurement (control hypertension)
- Random blood sugar/HbA1c (optimize diabetes - ideally RBS <200 mg/dL, though not absolute contraindication)
- Bleeding time, clotting time (if on anticoagulants)
- ECG (in elderly, cardiac patients)
- Chest X-ray (if respiratory symptoms)
- Physician/cardiologist clearance in high-risk patients

#### 2. Laboratory Investigations
Not routinely required but consider:
- Complete blood count
- Blood sugar levels
- Renal function tests (if diabetic)
- HIV, HBsAg (for staff safety in endemic areas)

### C. PRE-OPERATIVE PREPARATIONS

#### 1. Patient Education and Counseling
- Explain procedure, risks, benefits, alternatives
- Realistic expectation setting regarding visual outcome
- Discuss IOL options: monofocal, multifocal, toric
- Explain need for glasses post-operatively (especially with monofocal IOL)
- Obtain informed written consent

#### 2. Medication Adjustments
- **Anticoagulants/antiplatelets**: Generally continued (risk of hemorrhage vs thromboembolic events - discuss with physician). Aspirin usually not stopped for routine phacoemulsification
- **α-blockers** (tamsulosin): Inform surgeon - risk of Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome (IFIS)
- **Diabetic medications**: Take morning dose with sip of water, adjust insulin as per physician

#### 3. Pre-operative Medications (Day Before/Day of Surgery)

**Antibiotics**
- Topical antibiotic eye drops (moxifloxacin 0.5%, gatifloxacin 0.3%): Started 1-3 days before surgery, QID
- Purpose: Reduce bacterial load on ocular surface

**Mydriatics/Cycloplegics** (Day of Surgery)
- Tropicamide 0.8% + Phenylephrine 5%
- Started 30-60 minutes before surgery
- Multiple instillations every 10-15 minutes for adequate pupil dilation (≥6-7 mm)
- Add cyclopentolate 1% if needed

**NSAIDs**
- Topical NSAIDs (nepafenac, bromfenac, ketorolac): Started 1-3 days pre-op
- Purpose: Reduce inflammation, prevent cystoid macular edema

**Other Pre-operative Measures**
- NPO (nil per oral) 6-8 hours before surgery if GA planned
- Light meal allowed if local anesthesia
- Bath and clean clothes
- Remove dentures, jewelry
- Empty bladder before surgery

#### 4. Anesthesia Planning
**Options:**
- **Topical anesthesia**: Most common for phacoemulsification (proparacaine, lidocaine gel). Patient cooperation required
- **Peribulbar block**: Longer duration, better akinesia, safer than retrobulbar
- **Retrobulbar block**: Rarely used now, risk of complications
- **General anesthesia**: Children, anxious patients, mentally challenged, long surgical time expected

### D. DAY OF SURGERY CHECKLIST

**Pre-operative Checklist:**
- Verify correct patient identity, eye to be operated (signed and marked)
- Confirm IOL power and type
- Review medical clearance
- Check pupil dilation adequate
- Instill antibiotic and NSAID drops
- Povidone-iodine 5% to conjunctival sac (gold standard antisepsis - reduces endophthalmitis risk by 75%)
- Draping and preparation of surgical field
- Anesthesia administration
- Final timeout before incision

### E. CONTRAINDICATIONS TO CATARACT SURGERY

**Absolute:**
- Active ocular infection (conjunctivitis, dacryocystitis, endophthalmitis, corneal ulcer) - treat first
- Uncontrolled glaucoma
- Severe bleeding diathesis

**Relative:**
- Uncontrolled diabetes (optimize first, though not absolute contraindication)
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- Recent MI (wait 6 months ideally)
- Pregnancy (avoid in first trimester)
- Unrealistic patient expectations
- Patient unable to cooperate/lie flat

## KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

1. **Most important pre-op investigation**: Biometry for IOL power calculation
2. **Most important antisepsis measure**: Povidone-iodine 5% to conjunctival sac
3. **Most common type of cataract**: Age-related (senile)
4. **Most common drug causing cataract**: Corticosteroids (PSC type)
5. **Floppy Iris Syndrome**: Associated with tamsulosin (α-blocker) use
6. **TORCH infections**: Important cause of congenital cataract
7. **IOL calculation formulas**: SRK-T (most versatile), Haigis, Barrett (highly accurate)
8. **Target refraction**: Usually slight myopia (-0.5 to -1.0 D) in one eye for near work if monofocal IOL planned
9. **Second eye surgery**: Usually performed 1-2 weeks after first eye (though immediate sequential bilateral cataract surgery - ISBCS - gaining acceptance)
10. **Post-operative medications**: Topical antibiotics + steroids + NSAIDs (standard regimen)

---

*Remember: Successful cataract surgery outcome depends not just on surgical skill but on thorough pre-operative evaluation, appropriate patient selection, accurate biometry, and realistic expectation setting.*
