# Cataract: Morphological and Etiological Classification & Pre-operative Procedures

## MORPHOLOGICAL CLASSIFICATION OF CATARACT

### Based on Maturity
**1. Immature Cataract**
- Partially opaque lens with some clear areas
- Iris shadow present on oblique illumination
- Patient has some useful vision
- Can see fundus details on ophthalmoscopy

**2. Mature Cataract**
- Entire lens cortex is opaque
- No iris shadow on oblique illumination
- Vision reduced to PL (perception of light) or HM (hand movements)
- Cannot see fundus - absent red reflex

**3. Hypermature Cataract**
- Advanced stage of mature cataract
- Two subtypes:
  - **Morgagnian cataract**: Liquefied cortex with brown nucleus settling inferiorly, giving "bag of water" appearance
  - **Shrunken cataract**: Wrinkled, shrunken capsule with calcium deposits, reduced lens size

**4. Intumescent Cataract**
- Swollen lens due to water imbibition
- Increased lens thickness
- Shallow anterior chamber
- Risk of angle closure glaucoma (phacomorphic glaucoma)

### Based on Anatomical Location

**1. Cortical Cataract**
- Affects lens cortex
- Subtypes: cuneiform (wedge-shaped), cupuliform (saucer-shaped)
- Usually age-related

**2. Nuclear Cataract**
- Affects lens nucleus
- Progressive nuclear sclerosis with browning (brunescent) or black (nigra) coloration
- Common in elderly
- Causes myopic shift ("second sight")

**3. Subcapsular Cataract**
- **Posterior subcapsular (PSC)**: Most visually significant, causes glare, more symptomatic in bright light
- **Anterior subcapsular**: Less common, associated with trauma or inflammation

**4. Polar Cataract**
- Located at anterior or posterior pole
- Often congenital
- May be stationary or progressive

**5. Lamellar (Zonular) Cataract**
- Affects specific layer of lens
- Common congenital type
- Riders (club-shaped opacities) may be present

## ETIOLOGICAL CLASSIFICATION OF CATARACT

### 1. Age-Related (Senile) Cataract
- Most common type (>90% of cases)
- Usually bilateral but asymmetric
- Multifactorial: oxidative stress, UV exposure, protein denaturation
- Types: cortical, nuclear, PSC or mixed

### 2. Congenital Cataract
**Causes:**
- Intrauterine infections: TORCH (Toxoplasma, Rubella, CMV, HSV)
- Metabolic: galactosemia, galactokinase deficiency, Lowe syndrome
- Chromosomal: Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Turner syndrome
- Hereditary: autosomal dominant most common
- Idiopathic

**Morphological types:** lamellar, polar, total, sutural, membranous

### 3. Traumatic Cataract
- Penetrating injury: rosette cataract, total cataract
- Blunt trauma: Vossius ring (iris pigment imprint on anterior lens capsule)
- Electric shock, lightning, radiation
- May develop immediately or years later

### 4. Metabolic Cataract
- **Diabetic cataract**: True diabetic (snowflake cataract in young diabetics with poor control), accelerated senile changes
- **Hypocalcemia**: Hypoparathyroidism, causes subcapsular opacities with iridescent crystals
- **Wilson disease**: Sunflower cataract (copper deposition)
- **Myotonic dystrophy**: Christmas tree cataract (polychromatic crystals)

### 5. Complicated Cataract
Secondary to intraocular disease:
- Chronic anterior uveitis: posterior subcapsular, bread crumb appearance
- Retinitis pigmentosa
- High myopia
- Retinal detachment
- Glaucoma

### 6. Drug-Induced Cataract
- **Corticosteroids**: Posterior subcapsular cataract (most common drug-induced)
- **Phenothiazines**: Anterior subcapsular stellate opacities
- **Amiodarone**: Whorl-like deposits
- **Miotics** (long-term use)

### 7. Radiation Cataract
- Ionizing radiation: posterior subcapsular
- Infrared radiation: glassblower's cataract (exfoliation at anterior capsule)
- UV radiation: cortical cataract

### 8. Toxic Cataract
- Naphthalene poisoning
- Ergot poisoning
- Dinitrophenol

## PRE-OPERATIVE PROCEDURES FOR CATARACT SURGERY

### A. PRE-OPERATIVE ASSESSMENT

#### 1. History Taking
- Chief complaints: blurred vision, glare, difficulty with ADLs
- Duration and progression of symptoms
- Medical history: diabetes, hypertension, cardiac disease, bleeding disorders, medications (anticoagulants, α-blockers like tamsulosin - risk of floppy iris syndrome)
- Ocular history: previous surgery, trauma, uveitis
- Allergies
- Social history: occupation, visual needs, ability to comply with post-op care

#### 2. Ocular Examination

**Visual Acuity**
- Best corrected visual acuity (BCVA) - distance and near
- Pinhole vision
- Potential acuity testing if view of fundus is poor

**External Examination**
- Eyelids: blepharitis, ectropion, entropion (requires correction first)
- Lacrimal system: check for dacryocystitis (contraindication - treat first)
- Conjunctiva: infection, pterygium

**Slit Lamp Examination**
- Cornea: clarity, dystrophies, scars, endothelial status (specular microscopy if needed)
- Anterior chamber: depth (risk of angle closure), cells/flare
- Iris: synechiae, pseudo-exfoliation, pupil size and dilation
- Lens: type of cataract, posterior capsule status, zonular integrity

**Pupillary Examination**
- RAPD (relative afferent pupillary defect) - indicates optic nerve pathology
- Pupil dilation - if poor, may need iris hooks or pupil expansion devices

**Intraocular Pressure**
- Rule out glaucoma
- Pre-existing glaucoma may affect surgical planning

**Fundus Examination**
- Dilated fundoscopy: retinal pathology, macular status, disc evaluation
- If view is poor: B-scan ultrasonography to rule out retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, tumors

**Special Investigations**
- **Biometry** (most important pre-op investigation):
  - Optical biometry (IOLMaster, Lenstar) - preferred, more accurate
  - A-scan ultrasound biometry - if optical biometry not possible
  - Measures: axial length, anterior chamber depth, lens thickness, keratometry
  - IOL power calculation using formulas: SRK-T, Haigis, Barrett, Hoffer Q (selection depends on axial length)

- **Keratometry**: Corneal curvature measurement

- **B-scan ultrasonography**: If fundus not visible, to assess posterior segment

- **Specular microscopy**: Endothelial cell count (especially in Fuchs' dystrophy, previous surgery)

- **Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)**: Macular pathology assessment if fundus visible

- **Potential Acuity Meter (PAM)** or **Laser Interferometry**: Predict post-operative visual outcome when view of retina is obscured

### B. SYSTEMIC EVALUATION

#### 1. Medical Clearance
- General physical examination
- Blood pressure measurement (control hypertension)
- Random blood sugar/HbA1c (optimize diabetes - ideally RBS <200 mg/dL, though not absolute contraindication)
- Bleeding time, clotting time (if on anticoagulants)
- ECG (in elderly, cardiac patients)
- Chest X-ray (if respiratory symptoms)
- Physician/cardiologist clearance in high-risk patients

#### 2. Laboratory Investigations
Not routinely required but consider:
- Complete blood count
- Blood sugar levels
- Renal function tests (if diabetic)
- HIV, HBsAg (for staff safety in endemic areas)

### C. PRE-OPERATIVE PREPARATIONS

#### 1. Patient Education and Counseling
- Explain procedure, risks, benefits, alternatives
- Realistic expectation setting regarding visual outcome
- Discuss IOL options: monofocal, multifocal, toric
- Explain need for glasses post-operatively (especially with monofocal IOL)
- Obtain informed written consent

#### 2. Medication Adjustments
- **Anticoagulants/antiplatelets**: Generally continued (risk of hemorrhage vs thromboembolic events - discuss with physician). Aspirin usually not stopped for routine phacoemulsification
- **α-blockers** (tamsulosin): Inform surgeon - risk of Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome (IFIS)
- **Diabetic medications**: Take morning dose with sip of water, adjust insulin as per physician

#### 3. Pre-operative Medications (Day Before/Day of Surgery)

**Antibiotics**
- Topical antibiotic eye drops (moxifloxacin 0.5%, gatifloxacin 0.3%): Started 1-3 days before surgery, QID
- Purpose: Reduce bacterial load on ocular surface

**Mydriatics/Cycloplegics** (Day of Surgery)
- Tropicamide 0.8% + Phenylephrine 5%
- Started 30-60 minutes before surgery
- Multiple instillations every 10-15 minutes for adequate pupil dilation (≥6-7 mm)
- Add cyclopentolate 1% if needed

**NSAIDs**
- Topical NSAIDs (nepafenac, bromfenac, ketorolac): Started 1-3 days pre-op
- Purpose: Reduce inflammation, prevent cystoid macular edema

**Other Pre-operative Measures**
- NPO (nil per oral) 6-8 hours before surgery if GA planned
- Light meal allowed if local anesthesia
- Bath and clean clothes
- Remove dentures, jewelry
- Empty bladder before surgery

#### 4. Anesthesia Planning
**Options:**
- **Topical anesthesia**: Most common for phacoemulsification (proparacaine, lidocaine gel). Patient cooperation required
- **Peribulbar block**: Longer duration, better akinesia, safer than retrobulbar
- **Retrobulbar block**: Rarely used now, risk of complications
- **General anesthesia**: Children, anxious patients, mentally challenged, long surgical time expected

### D. DAY OF SURGERY CHECKLIST

**Pre-operative Checklist:**
- Verify correct patient identity, eye to be operated (signed and marked)
- Confirm IOL power and type
- Review medical clearance
- Check pupil dilation adequate
- Instill antibiotic and NSAID drops
- Povidone-iodine 5% to conjunctival sac (gold standard antisepsis - reduces endophthalmitis risk by 75%)
- Draping and preparation of surgical field
- Anesthesia administration
- Final timeout before incision

### E. CONTRAINDICATIONS TO CATARACT SURGERY

**Absolute:**
- Active ocular infection (conjunctivitis, dacryocystitis, endophthalmitis, corneal ulcer) - treat first
- Uncontrolled glaucoma
- Severe bleeding diathesis

**Relative:**
- Uncontrolled diabetes (optimize first, though not absolute contraindication)
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- Recent MI (wait 6 months ideally)
- Pregnancy (avoid in first trimester)
- Unrealistic patient expectations
- Patient unable to cooperate/lie flat

## KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

1. **Most important pre-op investigation**: Biometry for IOL power calculation
2. **Most important antisepsis measure**: Povidone-iodine 5% to conjunctival sac
3. **Most common type of cataract**: Age-related (senile)
4. **Most common drug causing cataract**: Corticosteroids (PSC type)
5. **Floppy Iris Syndrome**: Associated with tamsulosin (α-blocker) use
6. **TORCH infections**: Important cause of congenital cataract
7. **IOL calculation formulas**: SRK-T (most versatile), Haigis, Barrett (highly accurate)
8. **Target refraction**: Usually slight myopia (-0.5 to -1.0 D) in one eye for near work if monofocal IOL planned
9. **Second eye surgery**: Usually performed 1-2 weeks after first eye (though immediate sequential bilateral cataract surgery - ISBCS - gaining acceptance)
10. **Post-operative medications**: Topical antibiotics + steroids + NSAIDs (standard regimen)

---

*Remember: Successful cataract surgery outcome depends not just on surgical skill but on thorough pre-operative evaluation, appropriate patient selection, accurate biometry, and realistic expectation setting.*



# Retinal Function Tests and Additional Pre-operative Investigations for Cataract Surgery

## IMPORTANCE OF RETINAL FUNCTION ASSESSMENT

When a dense cataract prevents adequate fundus visualization, we need to assess the functional status of the posterior segment (retina, optic nerve, vitreous) to:
- Predict post-operative visual outcome
- Detect co-existing retinal pathology
- Counsel patients appropriately about realistic expectations
- Decide on surgical feasibility

---

## RETINAL FUNCTION TESTS

### 1. PROJECTION OF RAYS (PR)

**Principle:** Tests the functional integrity of different quadrants of the retina

**Method:**
- Patient seated in dark room
- Narrow beam of light (penlight/indirect ophthalmoscope) projected from different directions (superior, inferior, nasal, temporal)
- Ask patient to indicate direction from which light is coming

**Interpretation:**
- **Accurate PR in all quadrants**: Retina functioning normally in all areas
- **Inaccurate PR in one/more quadrants**: Suggests retinal pathology in that area (detachment, hemorrhage, ischemia)

**Grading:**
- PR accurate: Good prognosis
- PR inaccurate: Poor prognosis for that quadrant
- PR negative: Very poor prognosis

**Limitations:**
- Subjective test
- Requires patient cooperation
- Not reliable in confused/elderly patients
- Cannot detect macular pathology specifically

---

### 2. PERCEPTION OF LIGHT (PL)

**Principle:** Tests basic retinal and optic nerve function

**Method:**
- Shine bright light into the eye
- Ask if patient can perceive the light

**Grading:**
- **PL present**: Indicates some retinal/optic nerve function
- **PL absent**: Indicates complete loss of function (total retinal detachment, optic atrophy, absolute glaucoma)

**Clinical Significance:**
- PL absent = Poor surgical candidate
- Surgery may still be done for cosmetic reasons or to prevent complications (phacomorphic glaucoma)
- Patient must be counseled that vision will not improve

---

### 3. COLOUR PERCEPTION TEST

**Principle:** Tests cone function (macular function)

**Method:**
- Show colored objects (red, green) to patient
- Ask patient to identify colors

**Interpretation:**
- Accurate color identification: Suggests intact macular function
- Inability to identify colors: Macular pathology possible

**Limitations:**
- Cannot be used in color-blind patients
- Not standardized
- Subjective

---

### 4. ENTOPTIC PHENOMENA TESTS

#### a) **Purkinje's Phenomenon (Two-Point Test)**

**Principle:** Tests macular function by eliciting entoptic images

**Method:**
- Use two light sources placed close together
- Shine obliquely through pupil across lens opacity
- Ask patient if they see two separate light points

**Interpretation:**
- Seeing two separate points: Good macular function
- Seeing single blurred light: Macular pathology possible

**Limitation:** Difficult to perform, not widely used

#### b) **Maddox Rod Test**

**Principle:** Tests macular function

**Method:**
- Place Maddox rod (series of parallel cylindrical rods) before the eye
- Point source of light appears as linear streak perpendicular to rod orientation
- If macula functioning, patient sees the streak

**Interpretation:**
- Streak seen: Macular function intact
- No streak/distorted: Macular dysfunction

---

### 5. PHOTOSTRESS TEST (Glare Recovery Test)

**Principle:** Tests macular recovery time after exposure to bright light

**Method:**
1. Check baseline visual acuity
2. Shine bright light (penlight/direct ophthalmoscope) directly into eye for 10 seconds
3. Measure time taken for vision to return to within one line of baseline acuity

**Interpretation:**
- **Normal**: Recovery within 30-60 seconds
- **Macular pathology** (macular degeneration, macular edema): Delayed recovery (>1 minute)
- **Cataract alone**: Normal recovery time

**Clinical Significance:**
- Helps differentiate decreased vision due to cataract vs macular disease
- Prolonged recovery = poor post-operative visual prognosis

---

## OBJECTIVE TESTS FOR RETINAL FUNCTION

### 1. LASER INTERFEROMETRY (Potential Acuity Meter - PAM)

**Principle:** Projects interference fringes (alternate light and dark bands) directly onto retina, bypassing media opacities

**Method:**
- Two coherent laser beams create interference pattern on retina
- Patient asked to identify orientation of fringes (vertical/horizontal)
- Progressively narrower fringe widths tested
- Narrowest fringe detected correlates with potential visual acuity

**Interpretation:**
- Estimates potential post-operative visual acuity
- If patient sees fine fringes: Good macular function, good prognosis
- Cannot see fringes: Macular pathology, poor prognosis

**Advantages:**
- Objective
- Bypasses media opacities
- Good predictor of post-op vision

**Limitations:**
- Expensive equipment
- Not available everywhere
- Less reliable if very dense cataract
- May overestimate vision in some cases

---

### 2. ELECTROPHYSIOLOGICAL TESTS

#### a) **Electroretinography (ERG)**

**Principle:** Records electrical response of retina to light stimulus

**Components:**
- **a-wave**: Photoreceptor response (rods and cones)
- **b-wave**: Müller and bipolar cell response

**Types:**
- **Full-field (flash) ERG**: Tests global retinal function
- **Pattern ERG**: Tests macular function
- **Multifocal ERG**: Tests focal retinal areas

**Method:**
- Contact lens electrode placed on cornea
- Reference electrode on forehead
- Light stimulus given
- Electrical response recorded

**Interpretation:**
- **Normal ERG**: Good retinal function, good prognosis
- **Absent/reduced ERG**: 
  - Retinal dystrophies (retinitis pigmentosa)
  - Extensive retinal detachment
  - Central retinal artery occlusion
  - Poor prognosis

**Clinical Use:**
- Most useful for detecting widespread retinal pathology
- Less sensitive for macular-specific diseases
- Often combined with VEP

#### b) **Visual Evoked Potential (VEP)**

**Principle:** Records electrical response of visual cortex to visual stimulus

**Method:**
- Electrodes placed on occipital scalp
- Pattern or flash stimulus given
- Cortical response recorded (P100 wave most important)

**Interpretation:**
- **Normal VEP**: Intact visual pathway from retina to cortex
- **Abnormal VEP**: 
  - Prolonged latency: Demyelination (optic neuritis, multiple sclerosis)
  - Reduced amplitude: Optic atrophy, compressive lesions
  - Absent response: Severe optic nerve damage

**Flash VEP vs Pattern VEP:**
- **Flash VEP**: Can be done even with dense cataract, but less specific
- **Pattern VEP**: More specific for macular function, requires adequate media clarity

**Clinical Significance:**
- Evaluates entire visual pathway
- Detects optic nerve pathology
- Useful when cataract is very dense

---

### 3. ULTRASOUND B-SCAN

**Principle:** Uses high-frequency sound waves to create 2D image of posterior segment

**Indications:**
- Dense cataract preventing fundus view
- Vitreous hemorrhage
- Suspected retinal detachment
- Intraocular mass/tumor
- Posterior capsule rupture assessment

**Findings:**
- **Retinal detachment**: Mobile, highly reflective membrane attached at ora serrata and optic disc
- **Vitreous hemorrhage**: Low to medium reflective opacities in vitreous cavity
- **Posterior vitreous detachment**: Mobile membrane not attached posteriorly
- **Intraocular tumor**: Solid, dome-shaped mass
- **Endophthalmitis**: Thick vitreous echoes, membranes
- **Normal posterior segment**: Clear vitreous, attached retina

**Types:**
- **Standardized B-scan**: Fixed settings for comparison
- **A-scan**: Used primarily for biometry, can detect gross pathology

**Clinical Significance:**
- **ESSENTIAL investigation** when fundus not visible
- Helps detect contraindications to surgery (active retinal detachment, endophthalmitis)
- Guides surgical planning

---

### 4. OPTICAL COHERENCE TOMOGRAPHY (OCT)

**Principle:** Uses light waves to create cross-sectional images of retina

**Utility in Pre-operative Assessment:**
- Can penetrate mild to moderate cataracts
- Assesses macular thickness and structure
- Detects:
  - **Macular edema** (diabetic, post-uveitis)
  - **Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)**
  - **Epiretinal membrane (ERM)**
  - **Macular hole**
  - **Central serous retinopathy**

**Interpretation:**
- Normal macular contour and thickness: Good prognosis
- Macular edema, ERM, AMD: Guarded prognosis
- Full-thickness macular hole: Poor central vision prognosis

**Limitation:**
- Cannot penetrate dense cataracts
- If fundus not visible clinically, OCT usually not possible

---

### 5. SPECULAR MICROSCOPY

**Principle:** Assesses corneal endothelial cell count and morphology

**Indications:**
- Previous intraocular surgery
- Corneal dystrophies (Fuchs' dystrophy)
- Prolonged contact lens wear
- Suspected endothelial dysfunction
- High myopia
- Complicated cataract surgery risk assessment

**Normal Values:**
- Endothelial cell density: 2000-2500 cells/mm² (decreases with age)
- Coefficient of variation (CV): <0.30 (cell size variation)
- Hexagonality: >60%

**Interpretation:**
- **ECD >1500 cells/mm²**: Safe for routine phacoemulsification
- **ECD 1000-1500 cells/mm²**: Proceed with caution, gentle surgery
- **ECD <1000 cells/mm²**: High risk of corneal decompensation, consider ECCE or manage cornea first

**Clinical Significance:**
- Phacoemulsification causes endothelial cell loss (typically 10-15%)
- Low pre-op count increases risk of post-op corneal edema/decompensation
- May need penetrating keratoplasty (PK) or endothelial keratoplasty in future

---

### 6. CORNEAL TOPOGRAPHY/TOMOGRAPHY

**Purpose:**
- Map corneal curvature and shape
- Detect irregular astigmatism
- Screen for keratoconus or corneal ectasia

**Importance in Cataract Surgery:**
- Accurate keratometry readings for IOL calculation
- Planning for toric IOL if significant astigmatism
- Detect forme fruste keratoconus
- Guide incision placement

**Types:**
- Placido-based topography
- Scheimpflug imaging (Pentacam)
- Anterior segment OCT

---

### 7. POTENTIAL ACUITY METER (PAM)

**Principle:** Projects eye chart directly onto retina through small clear area

**Method:**
- Small pinhole beam of light carries miniature Snellen chart
- Projected through least dense area of cataract onto retina
- Patient reads the chart

**Interpretation:**
- Reading correlates with potential post-operative acuity
- PAM 20/40 or better: Good prognosis
- PAM 20/200 or worse: Poor prognosis, possible retinal pathology

**Advantages:**
- Simple, quick
- No special expertise needed
- Correlates well with post-op vision in many cases

**Limitations:**
- Requires some media clarity
- May overestimate in dense cataracts
- Patient cooperation required

---

## ADDITIONAL PRE-OPERATIVE CONSIDERATIONS

### 1. PUPIL EVALUATION

**Assessment:**
- Pupil size in dim and bright light
- Dilating capacity
- Iris abnormalities

**Special Situations:**

**Posterior Synechiae:**
- Adhesions between iris and lens
- Common in uveitis
- May need synechiolysis pre-operatively or intra-operatively

**Pseudo-exfoliation Syndrome:**
- White flaky material on lens capsule and iris
- Weak zonules - risk of zonular dialysis
- Poor pupil dilation
- May need capsular tension ring (CTR)

**Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome (IFIS):**
- Associated with α-blockers (tamsulosin)
- Triad: billowing iris, progressive miosis, iris prolapse
- Requires modified surgical technique
- Use iris hooks, Malyugin ring, or intracameral epinephrine

**Small Pupil:**
- If <6mm dilation, increased surgical difficulty
- Causes: diabetes, posterior synechiae, pseudo-exfoliation, miotic drops
- May need pupil expansion devices or iris hooks

---

### 2. ANTERIOR CHAMBER DEPTH ASSESSMENT

**Methods:**
- Van Herick test (slit lamp)
- Optical biometry (IOLMaster)
- Anterior segment OCT
- Ultrasound biomicroscopy (UBM)

**Significance:**
- **Shallow AC** (<2.5mm): 
  - Risk of angle closure
  - Risk of phacomorphic glaucoma
  - Surgical difficulty increased
  - May need different IOL (lower power, anterior chamber IOL)

- **Deep AC**: Consider Marfan syndrome, homocystinuria (risk of zonular weakness)

---

### 3. ZONULAR INTEGRITY ASSESSMENT

**Signs of Zonular Weakness:**
- Phacodonesis (lens wobbling on eye movement)
- Iridodonesis (iris wobbling)
- Decentered lens
- History of trauma
- Pseudo-exfoliation
- High myopia
- Marfan syndrome, homocystinuria

**Implications:**
- Increased risk of:
  - Posterior capsule rupture
  - Nucleus drop
  - IOL dislocation
- May need capsular tension ring (CTR) or capsular hooks
- ECCE may be safer than phacoemulsification in severe cases

---

### 4. CORNEAL PACHYMETRY

**Purpose:** Measures central corneal thickness (CCT)

**Normal CCT:** 540-560 μm

**Significance:**
- Thick cornea: May underestimate IOP (false low)
- Thin cornea: May overestimate IOP (false high), risk in glaucoma suspects
- Guides glaucoma management if co-existing
- Baseline for post-op monitoring

---

## ALGORITHM FOR PRE-OPERATIVE RETINAL ASSESSMENT

```
Clear Fundus View?
├─ YES → Direct/indirect ophthalmoscopy
│         ├─ Normal fundus → Proceed with surgery
│         └─ Abnormal fundus → OCT, treat pathology if needed
│
└─ NO → Dense cataract obstructing view
          ├─ B-scan USG (MANDATORY)
          │   ├─ Normal → Proceed with additional tests
          │   └─ Abnormal (RD, mass) → Manage pathology first
          │
          ├─ Projection of Rays
          │   ├─ Accurate in all quadrants → Good prognosis
          │   └─ Inaccurate → Consider combined surgery
          │
          ├─ Photostress Test (if cooperation possible)
          │   ├─ Normal recovery → Good macular function
          │   └─ Delayed → Guarded prognosis
          │
          └─ If available:
              ├─ ERG (for global retinal function)
              ├─ VEP (for optic pathway)
              └─ Laser interferometry/PAM (for potential acuity)
```

---

## SPECIAL SITUATIONS REQUIRING ADDITIONAL INVESTIGATIONS

### 1. **Diabetic Patients**
- **OCT** macula (if visible) - detect diabetic macular edema (DME)
- **B-scan** - vitreous hemorrhage, tractional RD
- **Pre-treat DME** with anti-VEGF or laser before surgery
- Optimize blood sugar (HbA1c <7-8%)

### 2. **High Myopes**
- **B-scan** - peripheral retinal breaks, RD
- **Dilated fundoscopy** - lattice degeneration, breaks
- **Consider prophylactic laser** to retinal breaks
- **Biometry** - use appropriate IOL formula (Haigis, Barrett)

### 3. **Post-Uveitis Cataract**
- **Control inflammation** (no cells in AC for 3 months minimum)
- **OCT** - cystoid macular edema
- **B-scan** - vitreous opacities, membranes
- Pre-treat with intensive topical/systemic steroids

### 4. **Traumatic Cataract**
- **B-scan** - IOFB, retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage
- **X-ray orbit** (if metallic IOFB suspected)
- **CT scan** - for localization of IOFB
- **Gonioscopy** - angle recession, cyclodialysis cleft
- **Assess zonular integrity**

### 5. **Pseudo-exfoliation**
- **Gonioscopy** - assess angle, pigment deposition
- **Assess zonular integrity** - phacodonesis
- **IOP measurement** - often associated with glaucoma
- Plan for CTR use

---

## KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

1. **Most important objective test when fundus not visible**: B-scan ultrasonography (MANDATORY)

2. **Simplest bedside tests**: Projection of rays, perception of light, photostress test

3. **Best predictor of post-op vision**: Laser interferometry/PAM (when available)

4. **ERG**: Tests global retinal function (photoreceptors, outer retina)

5. **VEP**: Tests entire visual pathway including optic nerve

6. **Specular microscopy**: Essential if endothelial dysfunction suspected

7. **If B-scan shows retinal detachment**: Cataract surgery should be combined with or preceded by RD repair

8. **Dense cataract with PL negative**: Poor surgical candidate, counsel for poor outcome

9. **Pre-operative optimization**:
   - Diabetics: Control sugar, treat DME
   - Uveitis: Control inflammation for 3 months
   - High myopes: Treat retinal breaks
   - Glaucoma: Control IOP

10. **"The quality of cataract surgery outcome depends as much on what happens before surgery as what happens during surgery"**

---

## SUMMARY TABLE: WHEN TO USE WHICH TEST

| Clinical Scenario | Recommended Tests |
|-------------------|------------------|
| **Dense cataract, fundus not visible** | B-scan (mandatory), PR, PL, ERG/VEP if available |
| **Mild cataract, fundus partially visible** | Dilated fundoscopy, OCT if macular concern |
| **Diabetic patient** | OCT macula, B-scan, HbA1c |
| **High myope** | B-scan, dilated retinal exam, appropriate IOL formula |
| **Suspected corneal endothelial dysfunction** | Specular microscopy |
| **Significant astigmatism** | Corneal topography, consider toric IOL |
| **Previous uveitis** | OCT macula, B-scan, ensure inflammation controlled |
| **Pseudo-exfoliation** | Gonioscopy, IOP, assess zonules, plan CTR |
| **Poor pupil dilation** | Identify cause (IFIS, synechiae), plan pupil devices |
| **Shallow anterior chamber** | AC depth measurement, consider angle closure risk |

*Remember: Thorough pre-operative assessment not only predicts visual outcome but also prevents complications and ensures appropriate patient counseling and surgical planning.*
