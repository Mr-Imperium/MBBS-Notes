

# **Comprehensive Approach to Fever and Rash in Pediatrics**

## **I. Introduction and Core Definitions**

In pediatric emergency medicine, the combination of fever and rash is one of the most common yet challenging presentations. The differential diagnosis spans from benign, self-limiting viral illnesses to rapidly fatal bacterial sepsis.

### **1. Fever Definitions (Chapter 119)**
Fever is a physiological response to infection, inflammation, or malignancy.
*   **The Threshold:** A temperature **≥38.0°C (100.4°F)** is considered a fever in infants.
*   **Measurement:**
    *   **Rectal:** The "gold standard" for infants <3 years. It most accurately reflects core temperature.
    *   **Axillary/Oral:** Generally 0.6°C (1°F) lower than rectal. Less reliable in screening for serious bacterial illness (SBI) in neonates.
*   **Hypothermia:** In neonates (<28 days), a temperature **<36.0°C (96.8°F)** is just as concerning as a fever and is a potential sign of sepsis.

### **2. Rash Morphology (Chapter 142)**
Describing the rash accurately narrows the diagnosis immediately.
*   **Exanthem:** A widespread rash on the skin accompanying a systemic disease.
*   **Enanthem:** A rash on mucous membranes (e.g., Koplik spots in the mouth).
*   **Maculopapular:** Flat red spots (macules) mixed with raised bumps (papules). Most common viral presentation.
*   **Vesicular:** Fluid-filled blisters (e.g., Chickenpox).
*   **Petechiae:** Small (<2mm), red/purple dots that **do not blanch** (do not fade with pressure). This signals bleeding under the skin.
*   **Purpura:** Larger (>2mm) non-blanching lesions. **Palpable purpura** implies vasculitis (inflammation of blood vessels).

## **II. The Critical Framework: Age-Stratified Risk**

**Chapter 119** emphasizes that **age** is the single most important factor in determining the risk of Serious Bacterial Illness (SBI).

### **1. The Neonate (0–28 Days): "High Risk"**
*   **Physiology:** Neonates have immature immune systems (decreased opsonization, poor neutrophil function). They cannot wall off infections.
*   **Clinical Presentation:** Signs are subtle. A septic neonate may only present with fever, hypothermia, or poor feeding.
*   **Key Pathogens:** Group B *Streptococcus*, *E. coli*, *Listeria monocytogenes*, Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV).
*   **Management:**
    *   **Mandatory:** Full Sepsis Workup (Blood culture, Urine catheter culture, **Lumbar Puncture** for CSF).
    *   **Treatment:** Admission + IV Antibiotics (Ampicillin + Cefotaxime OR Gentamicin).
    *   **Warning:** **Do not use Ceftriaxone** in neonates (displaces bilirubin, risk of kernicterus/biliary sludging).

### **2. The Young Infant (29–90 Days): "The Gray Zone"**
*   **Risk:** Still at risk for bacteremia and meningitis, but immune function is improving.
*   **Protocols:** Clinicians use criteria (Philadelphia, Rochester, Boston) to see if an infant is "low risk" enough to be managed as an outpatient.
*   **The "Low Risk" Infant:** Must appear well, have normal WBC counts, normal urinalysis, and reliable follow-up.
*   **Philadelphia Protocol:** The gold standard for sensitivity. If the infant fails *any* criteria, they often get a Lumbar Puncture and admission.

### **3. The Older Child (3 months – 3 years)**
*   **Risk:** Occult bacteremia is now rare (<1%) due to *HiB* and *Pneumococcal* vaccines.
*   **Primary Focus:**
    *   **Urinary Tract Infection (UTI):** The most common cause of fever without a source. Always check a urinalysis.
    *   **Viral Exanthems:** The most common cause of fever with rash.

## **III. Life-Threatening Emergencies ("The Do Not Miss")**

These conditions require immediate recognition and resuscitation.

### **1. Meningococcemia**
*   **Pathogen:** *Neisseria meningitidis*.
*   **The "Window of Opportunity":** Death can occur within hours. Early signs are nonspecific (leg pain, cold hands/feet, abnormal skin color).
*   **The Rash:** Starts as nonspecific blanching macules $\rightarrow$ progresses to **petechiae** (trunk/legs) $\rightarrow$ coalesces into **Purpura Fulminans** (large areas of necrosis/gangrene).
*   **Action:** Immediate IV antibiotics (Ceftriaxone) **before** lumbar puncture if the child is unstable. Do not delay for confirmation.

### **2. Kawasaki Disease**
*   **Pathology:** Systemic vasculitis (medium vessels). Major risk is **Coronary Artery Aneurysms**.
*   **Diagnostic Criteria (Classic):** Fever for **≥5 days** PLUS 4 of the following (**CRASH** mnemonic):
    *   **C**onjunctivitis (Bilateral, non-purulent/dry).
    *   **R**ash (Polymorphous – can look like anything, but *never* vesicular).
    *   **A**denopathy (Cervical, usually single node >1.5cm).
    *   **S**trawberry Tongue (Red, cracked lips, inflamed oral mucosa).
    *   **H**and/Foot changes (Swelling/edema in acute phase; peeling in subacute phase).
*   **Treatment:** IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin) + High-dose Aspirin. Early IVIG reduces aneurysm risk from 25% to <5%.

### **3. Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS)**
*   **Pathogen:** *Staphylococcus aureus* (produces an exfoliative toxin).
*   **Presentation:** Generalized tender erythema (skin looks scalded/burned).
*   **Key Exam Finding:** **Nikolsky Sign Positive** (gentle stroking of the skin causes the epidermis to separate/slough off).
*   **Differentiation:** Unlike Bullous Impetigo (which is localized), SSSS is widespread.
*   **Treatment:** Anti-staphylococcal antibiotics (e.g., Nafcillin, Oxacillin) + fluid management (similar to burn care).

### **4. Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / TEN**
*   **Pathology:** Severe hypersensitivity reaction, usually to drugs (antibiotics, anticonvulsants) or infections (*Mycoplasma*).
*   **Presentation:** Fever + Rash + **Mucosal Involvement** (at least 2 mucous membranes: eyes, mouth, genitals).
*   **Rash:** Targetoid lesions that progress to bullae and sloughing.
*   **Red Flag:** Skin pain out of proportion to exam.

## **IV. The Maculopapular Exanthems (Viral)**

These are the most common rashes. Differentiating them relies on the **constellation of symptoms** (prodrome) and the **pattern of spread**.

| Disease | Pathogen | Prodrome/Symptoms | Rash Characteristics | Key "Look For" |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Measles (Rubeola)** | Measles Virus | High Fever + **3 C's** (Cough, Coryza, Conjunctivitis) | **Cephalocaudal spread:** Starts at hairline/ears $\rightarrow$ face $\rightarrow$ trunk $\rightarrow$ feet. | **Koplik Spots:** White/blue-gray specks on buccal mucosa (pathognomonic). |
| **Rubella (German Measles)** | Rubella Virus | Low-grade fever, mild URI. | Similar to measles but fainter/pinker. Spreads rapidly (face $\rightarrow$ body) and clears rapidly (3 days). | **Forchheimer Spots** (petechiae on soft palate). **Posterior Auricular** lymphadenopathy. |
| **Roseola Infantum** | HHV-6 | **High Fever** (>39.5°C) for 3-5 days in a *well-appearing* infant. | **"Fever drops, Rash pops."** As the fever breaks, a pink rash appears on the **trunk** first. | The only rash that starts on the trunk and appears *after* defervescence. |
| **Erythema Infectiosum (Fifth Disease)** | Parvovirus B19 | Mild fever, runny nose. | **Stage 1:** "Slapped Cheek" red face.<br>**Stage 2:** Reticular ("lacy") rash on extremities. | Rash worsens with heat/sun. **Risk:** Hydrops fetalis in pregnant women; Aplastic crisis in Sickle Cell. |
| **Drug Eruption** | (Medication) | History of new drug (e.g., Amoxicillin) 7-10 days prior. | Morbilliform (measles-like) symmetrical rash. Itchy. | No viral prodrome. Resolves with stopping drug. |

## **V. Vesicular Rashes (Blisters)**

### **1. Varicella (Chickenpox)**
*   **Pathogen:** Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV).
*   **Morphology:** **"Teardrop on a rose petal"** (clear vesicle on an erythematous base).
*   **Evolution:** Lesions appear in crops. A hallmark sign is **Polymorphism**: seeing papules, vesicles, and crusted scabs all at the same time.
*   **Complications:** Bacterial superinfection (Group A Strep), Pneumonia (adults), Cerebellitis (ataxia).

### **2. Hand-Foot-and-Mouth Disease (HFMD)**
*   **Pathogen:** Coxsackievirus A16 or Enterovirus 71.
*   **Presentation:**
    1.  **Mouth:** Painful ulcers (herpangina) $\rightarrow$ child stops eating.
    2.  **Skin:** Greyish vesicles on palms, soles, and buttocks.
*   **Complications:** **Enterovirus 71** is a more virulent strain linked to rhombencephalitis (brainstem inflammation) and paralysis. Nail shedding (onychomadesis) can occur weeks later.

### **3. Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)**
*   **Gingivostomatitis:** High fever, swollen gums, painful oral vesicles. Dehydration is the main risk.
*   **Eczema Herpeticum:** A medical emergency where HSV superinfects eczema (atopic dermatitis). "Punched out" erosions. Requires IV Acyclovir.

## **VI. Bacterial & Vasculitic Rashes**

### **1. Scarlet Fever**
*   **Pathogen:** Group A *Streptococcus* (Strep Throat).
*   **Presentation:** Fever, sore throat, abdominal pain.
*   **The Rash:**
    *   **Texture:** "Sandpaper" feel.
    *   **Appearance:** Diffuse erythema (sunburn-like).
    *   **Pastia’s Lines:** Linear petechiae in skin folds (armpits/elbows).
    *   **Desquamation:** Peeling of fingers/toes occurs weeks later.
*   **Tongue:** "White Strawberry Tongue" (coated) $\rightarrow$ "Red Strawberry Tongue" (raw).
*   **Treatment:** Penicillin/Amoxicillin to prevent Rheumatic Fever.

### **2. Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)**
*   **Pathology:** IgA-mediated vasculitis. Most common vasculitis in children.
*   **Tetrad of Symptoms:**
    1.  **Rash:** **Palpable Purpura** (bruises you can feel), typically on buttocks and legs (gravity-dependent).
    2.  **Arthritis/Arthralgia:** Knees and ankles.
    3.  **Abdominal Pain:** Colicky pain (risk of intussusception).
    4.  **Renal Involvement:** Hematuria/Proteinuria (IgA nephropathy).
*   **Management:** Supportive (NSAIDs for pain). Steroids used only for severe abdominal or renal disease.
